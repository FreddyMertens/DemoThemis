Where Break the Court and the system design still disagree

The adaptive panel ladder is now broadly correct. The remaining high-priority problems are about which jurors count, how appeals are tested, and whether every public explanation is teaching the same rules.

7high-priority sync issues

None requires abandoning adaptive panels. They require the same frozen inputs, appeal logic, voting model, and public rules to be used everywhere.

The short answer

The foundation is aligned; the calculations around it are not yet fully shared

Already aligned

Both pages use the same five legal configurations: 7, 15, 31, 3 × 31, and 5 × 31. They use value to tighten a risk limit, test configurations in order, and select the smallest one that passes security, quality, formation, capacity, and funding.

Still unsafe to describe as one model

The two pages can feed that shared ladder different juror pools, fee assumptions, vote rules, and appeal assumptions. The shared ladder can therefore produce different answers depending on which page is explaining it.

High-priority issues

What is wrong, why it matters, and how to fix it

Each issue below can change the selected court, the quoted appeal, or the outcome a reviewer believes the protocol will produce.

01

The “frozen” juror pool changes while panel sizes are being tested

A candidate configuration can partly change the evidence used to declare itself safe.

High

In plain English: the protocol is supposed to decide who is eligible first, freeze that list, and then test every panel against the same list. Break the Court instead recalculates pay, workload, retention, and drawable jurors for each candidate.

System design

Freeze exposure and the conservative eligible cohort. Candidate-specific qualifications may remove people, but cannot create a larger safety pool.

Current Break model

Testing 31 seats and testing 5 × 31 seats can produce different retained and drawable populations because the candidate changes the economics.

Simple example
Start1,000 potentially eligible jurors
→
Test a small panelModel says 330 remain drawable
→
Test a large panelModel now says 350 are drawable

The underlying case and people did not change; only the candidate changed. That makes the comparison circular.

Why it matters

  • A larger configuration can pass using a pool that did not exist when testing began.
  • Capture probability and capacity can look better than the genuinely frozen case state supports.

Best fix

Build one case-frozen cohort before evaluating candidates. Calculate retention separately as a possible disqualifier, and cap every candidate’s usable cohort at the frozen number.

Where the mismatch appears

DemoThemis, “Choose the smallest ruling set that is actually safe,” says to freeze the cohort first and says fee-driven retention cannot inflate it. Break recalculates supply inside each candidate evaluation.

02

The next appeal is tested using the current court’s eligibility and pay assumptions

The lab changes the number of seats, but does not completely rebuild the next rung.

High

In plain English: a 15-seat court and a 31-seat court do not necessarily use the same eligible people or cost per juror. Break currently starts with the 15-seat context, subtracts the current jurors, and uses what remains to estimate the 31-seat appeal.

System design

Every appeal uses the immediate stronger rung, reapplies all qualification and prior-juror exclusions, prices every new seat, and halts if that fresh court cannot form.

Current Break model

The next appeal inherits the current rung’s drawable pool and per-vote pay. A 15 → 31 appeal can therefore count jurors who do not meet the 31-seat seniority requirement.

Why this can fail
Current 31-seat ruling set38 people needed with standbys; 92 appear available
→
After 31 serve61 appear fresh
→
Next 3 × 31 appeal112 fresh people are required

The current selector can still recommend the initial court even though the promised next appeal cannot form.

Why it matters

  • An appeal may be shown as cheaper or more available than it really is.
  • A case can be admitted with no viable next rung, silently weakening the appeal promise.
  • Subtracting only the current panel is wrong once a case has a longer served-juror history.

Best fix

Create a path-aware appeal calculation: actual served-juror history → next-rung qualifications → next-rung workload and fee → capacity and security gates. Either require the next rung at intake or explicitly declare the admitted ruling terminal.

Where the mismatch appears

Break computes the next candidate after finishing the current candidate model, then reuses the current drawable cohort and pay rate. DemoThemis requires exclusions and qualifications before testing every rung.

03

The attacker assumption becomes weaker when the appeal is priced

Initial selection and appeal security do not use one published threat model.

High

In plain English: the initial panel is tested against at least a published 25% attacker-weight scenario. The next appeal’s security amount is based on the smaller attacker share visible in the current lab settings. Lowering the attacker slider can therefore make an appeal look safer without changing the published security promise.

Initial panel

Uses the published 25% attacker-weight envelope, or a harsher visible attack when one exists.

Next appeal

Uses the current qualified coalition as a percentage of the current drawable pool. The DemoThemis appeal widget uses yet another assumption: up to 500 attackers.

The unintended result
Published promiseStress 25% attacker weight
→
Lab sliderVisible bloc happens to equal 4%
→
Appeal quoteSecurity is priced using 4%

The appeal becomes cheaper because the calculator changed assumptions, not because the system proved a safer pool.

Why it matters

The security bond can be understated, making repeated or strategic appeals cheaper than the published threat model intends.

Best fix

Publish one versioned appeal threat rule. Use the worst of the constitutional envelope and the current measured pressure, then use that same rule in Break, DemoThemis, and the run-through.

Where the mismatch appears

Break’s initial policy context inserts the published 25% envelope. Its next-appeal probability instead derives attackers from the current modeled bloc share. The DemoThemis appeal calculator uses a fixed 500-attacker ceiling.

04

The run-through still teaches the old panel-selection and consensus rules

A reviewer can learn one rule in Chapter 1 and see another in Break the Court.

High

In plain English: the run-through says money directly selects a panel tier and that parallel panels must all agree. The current adaptive design says value only tightens the risk requirement, and 2 of 3 or 3 of 5 panels can decide.

Run-through currently says

“Panel plan fixed by value.” Parallel top-tier panels must reach the same answer; a split becomes insufficient information.

Current shared policy says

Test every legal configuration and choose the smallest passing one. Two of three or three of five panel majorities produce the ruling.

Concrete contradiction
Panel A: YES
Panel B: YES
Panel C: NO

Under the shared policy, YES wins 2–1. Under the run-through wording, the disagreement becomes insufficient information. Both cannot be the production rule.

Why it matters

The run-through is the first and easiest explanation of the system. If it teaches the old rule, most reviewers will carry the wrong mental model into every later chapter.

Best fix

Rewrite those run-through steps: value tightens the risk limit; the selector chooses the first passing configuration; parallel configurations use 2-of-3 or 3-of-5 panel majorities.

Where the mismatch appears

Run-through lifecycle events “Panel plan fixed by value” and “Required panels agree?” conflict with the shared adaptive policy and the updated DemoThemis ladder.

05

The chapter describes weighted jurors, while Break counts equal people and equal votes

This is an unresolved design choice, not merely a different display.

High

In plain English: DemoThemis says reputation can make a juror up to three times as likely to be drawn and that full vote weight must be earned. Break calculates capture as if every eligible juror is equally likely to be selected and every selected seat has one equal vote.

DemoThemis chapter

Private reputation changes draw rate, pay, and vote weight. A person may be eligible without yet having full vote weight.

Break calculation

It converts attacker “weight” into an attacker headcount and applies an equal-probability finite-pool draw followed by a simple seat majority.

Why the words matter
Equal-seat system100 people = 100 equal chances and 100 equal votes
≠
Weighted system100 people can create different selection chances and different tally influence

An exact probability for the first system is not automatically exact for the second.

Why it matters

  • Capture odds may be optimistic or pessimistic depending on where high reputation is concentrated.
  • “One human, one vote” is also unclear if tally weight differs.

Best fix

Choose explicitly. The cleaner security design is one human, one equal seated vote, with reputation affecting qualification, draw frequency, and pay only. If weighted voting remains, Break must model weighted sampling and weighted tally capture.

Where the mismatch appears

DemoThemis, “The reputation dial,” specifies a draw-rate cap and earned full vote weight. Break and the shared panel policy use integer juror counts and ordinary majorities.

06

The complete court has three outcomes, but the robustness model is binary

Break cannot test attacks or failure modes that aim for no usable verdict.

High

In plain English: the product allows YES, NO, and INSUFFICIENT INFORMATION. Break reduces each juror or panel to “correct” or “wrong.” It therefore cannot show what happens when no option receives the required majority or when an attacker benefits by forcing an inconclusive result.

Complete product

INSUFFICIENT INFORMATION is a real ruling with its own market and escrow consequences.

Break model

Decision quality is a binary correct-majority probability. Attendance formation is measured, but verdict formation across three options is not.

A missing state
YES bloc
NO bloc
INSUFFICIENT bloc

The court needs a deterministic rule for an outright majority, a plurality, or no consensus. The current model does not distinguish these paths.

Why it matters

A denial-of-resolution attack can have economic value even when the attacker cannot force YES or NO. Binary capture probability does not measure that risk.

Best fix

Define the three-outcome tally and escalation rule first. Then include directional capture, forced-insufficient capture, and no-consensus probability in the selector. If this is deferred, label Break clearly as a binary abstraction.

Where the mismatch appears

OmenMarketMaker, DemoThemis, and the run-through all give insufficient information settlement meaning. Break and the shared decision-quality calculation currently use a binary majority model.

07

The two public panel calculators do not actually use the same reference inputs

One calculator can say a configuration passes while the other correctly halts it.

High

In plain English: the DemoThemis widget says it is demonstrating the same reproducible adaptive policy as Break. But it automatically marks fee funding as feasible, uses $1.50 per seat, and keeps 75% of the pool for senior panels. Break calculates the full fee and defaults to a 45% senior pool.

DemoThemis widget

Fee gate is always passed. Required fee is simplified to seats × $1.50. Senior eligibility is 75%.

Break model

Fee includes processing, adaptive panel pay, reward-pool top-up, and capped operations. Senior eligibility defaults to 45% before other reductions.

What a reviewer can see
Same visible caseExposure, pool, attacker share, accuracy
→
DemoThemisFunding always passes
≠
BreakFunding or senior capacity can halt

The shared ladder is not enough when each page supplies it with different facts.

Why it matters

The simpler chapter widget can recommend a court the full model says cannot be funded or staffed, undermining confidence in both explanations.

Best fix

Share a single versioned context builder for frozen cohort, senior qualification, deterministic fee, and attack scenarios. Both pages can present different levels of detail while calculating the same answer.

Where the mismatch appears

The DemoThemis adaptive widget hardcodes fee feasibility and a 75% senior cohort. Break uses the full deterministic fee and the assumptions file’s 45% senior-pool default.

Recommended repair order

Fix the shared calculations before polishing the explanations

This order prevents copy from being rewritten around rules that may still change.

1

Freeze one case context

Exposure, eligible cohort, served-juror history, and published threat assumptions become immutable inputs.

2

Build one appeal engine

Reapply next-rung qualifications, pricing, capacity, and security using the actual dispute path.

3

Resolve voting semantics

Choose equal or weighted seats and define exactly how three outcomes reach a ruling.

4

Connect every public surface

Use the shared context in both widgets, then update the run-through’s selection and consensus copy.

What “fully synchronized” should mean

  • The same case produces the same frozen cohort on every page.
  • The same next appeal produces the same capacity, fee, capture odds, and security amount.
  • Parallel panel results have one published decision rule.
  • YES, NO, and insufficient information are modeled consistently.
  • No public calculator silently bypasses a safety gate.
  • Break remains an explanatory stress test, not an alternative protocol specification.

Scope: high-priority synchronization issues between the reviewer-visible Break the Court lab and the public chapters. Lower-priority wording, governance-range, and presentation cleanup is intentionally left to the broader contradiction review.
