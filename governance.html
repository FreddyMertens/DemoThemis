Governance

Governance: a for-profit market token and a court vote money cannot buy

This chapter sets out PredictionMoMo's plain one-token-one-vote plutocracy, built for profit and an airdrop; demothemis's person-weighted vote that capital cannot concentrate; and the constitutional firewall that keeps the company's money from ever reaching the bench.

The tokens 11 min read • Act IV · Chapter 9 of 10

Two tokens, two ways of running things, on purpose. PredictionMoMo is governed like the company it is: one token, one vote, given out for free, openly out to make money. demothemis is governed like the public utility it must be: counted in verified people, multiplied by how much they serve. A firewall written into the rules keeps the money from ever reaching the bench.

The chapters before this one built a verdict nobody can buy. They also left dials everywhere: fees, curation bars, graduation thresholds, juror pay. Somebody has to turn those dials, and whoever turns them can, slowly, un-build everything else.

So the design splits the steering wheel in two, and the split follows one question: is the thing being governed a business, or the truth? PredictionMoMo is a business, deliberately. It exists to grow fast, take fees, and bankroll the court, so its token is plain plutocracy: one token, one vote, no personhood required, buyable by anyone with conviction and capital. demothemis is the truth machine, so its token is the opposite: weight counted in verified people, grown only by serving, immune to wallets. Between them sits a firewall written into the constitution, and the rest of this chapter is the two governments, the wall, and the numbers.

The company token

Plutocracy, on purpose

Every other page on this site treats one-token-one-vote as the original sin. So say plainly why it is the right government here: plutocracy is only a failure when the thing being governed is supposed to be neutral. PredictionMoMo is not supposed to be neutral. It is supposed to be a company in token form, growing fast, spending aggressively on its own adoption, and generating the revenue that subsidizes the arbitration service. Shareholders with more capital getting more say over a business is not a corruption of anything. It is what a business is, and pretending otherwise would just make the token less investible without making anything fairer.

What the token owns is the margin. The base 2% settlement fee and its 70/20/10 split are the court's invoice, constitutional, routed before a cent of profit exists. Above that base, the market token may vote itself a margin of up to one additional point on the fee, and that margin, plus the treasury, is what the token is: a claim on the growth of the busiest product in the system. Launch distribution is built for that growth: a genesis airdrop to early traders, sized by the same daily-net volume metric the old design used, gated by World ID so bot farms cannot farm it, and free-floating afterward, because after the airdrop, money is just money.

Now price the worst case, because that is how this site treats every mechanism. Suppose a single whale buys outright control of the market token. What can they actually do? Raise the margin to its 1% cap and tax their own product toward its competitors. Misspend the treasury. Lower the curation bar and embarrass themselves. What they cannot do, at any holding size, is touch a resolution, a juror, an answer key, the windows, the invoice, or the court's parameters, because none of those sit behind this token's vote. A captured company token is a badly run company, and badly run companies are survivable. That bounded blast radius is what makes it safe to let this token be exactly what investors want it to be.

The court token

One person's square root

The court's dials get the opposite government, because here a bought vote is a bought verdict one step removed. Weight in the court house is the square root of your court tokens, counted once per World ID. Square-root voting is an old, beautiful, broken idea: beautiful because voice grows slower than wealth, broken everywhere it has been tried because a whale splits one wallet into a thousand sock puppets and takes the root a thousand times. The math only works if you can count people, and this system can. Link as many wallets as you like; their tokens sum first, and the root applies once, to you. An unverified wallet can hold and trade the token freely but carries zero governance weight until a verified human claims it.

Splitting across unlinked wallets zeroes the whale's weight instead of multiplying it, because unverified wallets do not vote and linked wallets sum before the root. The 0.5% per-person cap is not even needed here; it exists for the day a whale is also a heavy user.

On top of the root sits a multiplier you can only earn by serving the court: 1 plus 1.5 times your escrow fees paid over (fees plus $50), plus 1.5 times your reputation-credited cases over (cases plus 30), saturating at 4. Fees are counted rather than gross escrow precisely so cycling money to yourself through a private room burns 2% per lap, and cases are counted by the credited score the hybrid system already keeps, so a juror who spam-clicks verdicts earns nothing here. Both inputs decay on a 90-day half-life, the half-saturation points track the live medians, and the result is capped at 0.5% of the house per person. Two boring rules finish the job: tokens must sit linked for 30 days before they count, and the snapshot locks at proposal creation, so a vote cannot be rented the night before the question.

Pricing the takeover

Illustrative: 50,000 verified holders, token at $0.10, 0.5% per-person cap. Buying alone hits the cap as one person. Faking service pays 2% of every faked escrow dollar into the fee split, where it funds the jurors and the reward pool. Recruiting means convincing thousands of verified humans, each of whom votes in the locked box and can take your money and vote their own mind.

The pattern is the court's capture math wearing a different shirt. One person, however rich, is a rounding error by construction: the root, then the cap. Faking service is worse than useless, because every faked dollar pays the fee that funds the thing being attacked. And the only strategy that scales, recruiting real verified people, is the same wall the court built: thousands of humans, each voting a ballot that cannot prove how it voted. Court governance ballots run through the same locked box as verdicts, so a vote-buying contract cannot verify delivery here either. The market token needs none of this machinery, and that is not an oversight: buying votes in a plutocracy is just called buying tokens, and the firewall is what makes that shrug affordable.

The rails

What a captured vote still could not do

The last defense assumes every other one failed, on either side. Every parameter moves only inside a hard-coded range, every passed proposal waits out a 7-day timelock in public, and the load-bearing rules are not parameters at all. No vote, in either house, at any margin, can reroute the invoice, cross the firewall, make court votes provable, make court weight linear, drop the personhood requirement, reopen settled escrow, or redirect slashes away from the reward pool. The constitution is the part of the machine with no dial on it.

The tokens themselves

Where each one comes from, and what each never does

The market token is built to be owned. One billion fixed: 30% to the community through the genesis airdrop and first-year usage streams, 25% to investors on a vest, 25% to the market treasury, 20% to the team over four years with a one-year cliff. It is the instrument for everyone who wants the growth: the airdrop seeds it into the hands of the traders who made the volume, and the float after that is a market like any other. Its claim is the margin and the treasury, and its job is blunt: grow a company fast enough to fund the bench forever.

The court token is built to be earned. One billion fixed: half streamed over eight years pro-rata to the same decayed service scores the multiplier reads, a fifth at genesis to the first year's escrow users and jurors, a fifth to a treasury the court house spends through the timelock, a tenth to the builders. It trades freely, because the defense lives in the weight function rather than transfer restrictions, and it never pays anyone. Juror wages, watcher bounties, and reward pool payouts all come from the invoice, exactly as the economics chapter prices them. One token chases profit so the other never has to: the moment the court's token printed salaries, every dial-turn would become a fight over the printer, and the printer is the one machine this design refuses to build on the bench's side of the wall.
