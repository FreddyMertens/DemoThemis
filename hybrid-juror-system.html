The build sheet

The hybrid juror system, in full

How a court can sort its good jurors from its bad ones without ever knowing the right answer. Built up from scratch, in plain English, one idea at a time.

Reputation systems 14 min read • Act II · Chapter 3 of 7

Here is a strange question. How do you grade a juror when nobody has the answer key?

A court exists to decide things that are not yet settled. So there is no teacher at the front of the room holding the correct answers, no way to mark a juror right or wrong against the truth, because the truth is the thing you are trying to produce. And yet you still want to keep the careful jurors and let go of the careless ones. That sounds impossible. It is not, and the trick behind it is the same trick that lets a crowd at a fair guess the weight of an ox better than most farmers can.

This page builds the answer one piece at a time. Three ideas hold the whole thing together, and every block below is one of them wearing a different costume. Judge a juror by their actual judging, never by a one-off test. Punish nobody until the numbers are sure. And quietly slip in a few questions you do know the answer to, so the crowd can never agree its way into a comfortable lie. The diagram is the finished machine. The rest of the page takes it apart.

Do not worry about the middle box yet. By the end of the page every word in it will feel obvious. Start with the question underneath everything.

The big idea

Why a crowd can beat its smartest member

In 1906 the statistician Francis Galton watched 787 people at a country fair guess the weight of an ox. Almost no one was close. But when he averaged all the guesses, the crowd landed within a pound of the true weight, beating every cattle expert in the room. The mistakes did not vanish. They cancelled. One person guessed too high, another too low, and across hundreds of guesses the errors pulled in opposite directions and erased each other. That is the wisdom of the crowd, and it is the engine the whole court runs on.

Now swap the ox for a verdict. Instead of guessing a weight, each juror answers yes or no, and instead of being a little high or low, each one is simply right a bit more often than a coin flip. The same cancelling still happens. Stack enough slightly-better-than-random jurors together and the majority is correct almost every time. That result has a name and a date: the Condorcet jury theorem, proved in 1785. Move the sliders and watch a panel of mediocre jurors turn into a near-perfect verdict.

This is the quiet miracle the court is built on. You do not need geniuses, and you do not need to know who the geniuses are. You only need a pile of ordinary people who lean toward the truth, picked at random so their errors stay independent. That random pick has a name too, sortition, and it is how Athens staffed its juries 2,400 years ago.

Block 1

Seven jurors, and nobody grades their own homework

If a bigger crowd is more accurate, why not summon a thousand jurors and settle every case to twelve decimal places? Because the crowd's payoff shrinks fast. Going from one juror to seven buys a huge leap in accuracy. Going from seven to seventy barely nudges it, while the bill and the waiting climb in a straight line the whole way. That pattern, a lot of benefit early and almost none later, is diminishing returns, and seven sits right at the bend.

So seven is the everyday panel. When a case is high-stakes or the vote comes back nearly tied, an appeal redraws a larger jury of fifteen, then thirty-one, paying for the last few points of certainty only where they are worth the cost.

Now the harder half. That same panel of seven has a second job, and it is the job that makes the whole reputation system possible. The votes that decide the case are also the only ruler you have for grading the jurors, because right and wrong are off the table. All you can measure is whether a juror moved with the room.

That sets a trap you have to step around carefully. Picture a four-to-three split. The three on the losing side are only counted wrong because four other people outvoted them, which tells you nothing about who actually had it right. Grade a juror against a verdict their own vote helped create and you are letting a student mark their own exam. The way out is a single rule: pull a juror out of the room before you judge them. Each one is scored against the leave-one-out majority, the answer the other six reach on their own. Match those six independent peers and you have an honest reading of whether a juror tracks the same truth the rest of the room is circling.

Block 2

Losing by an inch is not the same as a rout

A flaw sinks the simple version of this idea. If every disagreement is treated the same, the system is lying to itself about evidence. Picture each verdict as a tug-of-war. A seven to nothing result is a total rout, the rope dragged all the way to one side. If you pulled the other way on that case, you almost certainly missed something the whole room could see. A four to three result is the rope barely twitching around the center flag, a near tie that careful people landed on both sides of. Pulling the losing way there says almost nothing about you.

So every change to a juror's record is scaled by the margin of the vote. Lopsided verdicts move a reputation a lot, near ties barely move it. That is honest bookkeeping, trusting a clear signal and shrugging off a noisy one. It also kills two temptations at once. There is no reward for copying the crowd on coin-flip cases, since matching a bare majority earns almost nothing. And the careful holdout on a genuinely hard case, the very juror a crude score would chase away, is left alone.

Block 3

A reputation is a belief that comes into focus

A juror gets their first case right. Are they good? You have no idea. One correct call is one lucky coin flip. After fifty cases you start to know something, and after a few hundred you know them well. So a reputation here is not a single number stamped on a person. It is a belief about how accurate they are, and like any belief it should be fuzzy when the evidence is thin and sharp when the evidence piles up. Updating a belief by exactly this much with each new fact is the job of Bayesian reasoning, and the shape it tracks for a yes-or-no skill is called a beta distribution.

Picture a blurry photo coming into focus. A brand-new juror is a wide, low smear spread across the whole range, because their true skill could be almost anything. Each case they judge sharpens the picture a little. The diagram below is one juror photographed again and again, the blur tightening into a clear peak as the cases add up.

Holding three jurors up against a standard line shows why this matters. The newcomer is a wide hump, so the system trusts them a little and punishes them not at all. The strong juror is a narrow peak well above the line. The struggling juror is a narrow peak clearly below it. And because the picture only keeps the last fifty to a hundred and fifty cases, it has a short memory on purpose. A veteran who starts coasting blurs and slides down; someone who had a rough month can sharpen back up. Recent work counts, old work fades.

Block 4

A louder mic for reliable voices, with a volume cap

If you already believe one juror is right 95 percent of the time and another is barely better than a coin, why hand them an identical say? Tossing that knowledge away at a plain in-or-out line wastes it. So reputation becomes a volume knob. The more reliable a voice, the louder it speaks in the final tally. This is not a guess about fairness. The statistically best way to pool independent yes-or-no opinions is to weight each one by the log-odds of its reliability, which is the formal version of trust the steady voices more.

A knob with no ceiling is dangerous, though. A handful of veterans could crank themselves loud enough to decide cases alone, and the court would slide back into rule by a few, the exact thing one person, one vote was built to end. So the volume is capped. A reliable juror is heard a little louder than a shaky one, and never loud enough to drown out the room.

Block 5

Innocent until proven careless

Now the dangerous part, removing a juror. Get it wrong in one direction and you exile good people over a bad week. Get it wrong in the other and you keep bad ones forever. Courts already settled the principle long ago: you do not convict on a hunch, you convict only beyond a reasonable doubt. The same rule applies here. A juror is never suspended for a low best guess. They are suspended only once the entire range of belief about them, the whole error bar, has sunk below the standard. That error bar is a confidence interval, and waiting for all of it to clear the line is what keeps one unlucky streak from ending a career. While the bar still straddles the line, the juror is just building a record, drawn less and given a quieter mic, but never punished. Drag the sliders and watch where each juror lands.

Standard set at 70%. A juror is suspended only once the whole interval falls below it, which takes a real run of evidence, not a bad day.

Then there is the question of repeats. A first dip below the line buys a short timeout, about a week. Drop again and the pause grows, then grows more, like a penalty box that remembers how often you have been sent to it. The shape is the point. It stays gentle enough that an honest juror who slipped once pays almost nothing across a whole career, and steep enough that someone who keeps coasting is parked for longer and longer. The door never locks, though. A juror who returns and does well watches the penalty shrink again, so the pool can always refill from people who turned it around.

Block 6

The lone voice that turns out to be right

Every system that scores agreement shares one nightmare, the person who disagrees with everyone and turns out to be right. Score that juror on coherence alone and you punish them for being early, which slowly breeds the independent minds out of the pool. So the court keeps a slower second opinion in reserve. When a case is appealed, a larger and more careful panel looks again, and if it overturns the first verdict, the original holdouts were right all along. The system then reaches back in time and rewrites those scores. The juror who dissented has the provisional black mark erased and gets a bonus on top, while the overturned majority takes the margin-weighted hit they earned.

This is the move that tips the reward from being popular to being right. A juror who sees what the room missed no longer has to choose between an honest vote and their standing, because the appeal can still prove them correct later. Across many cases that keeps the brave and accurate minority in the pool, rather than quietly teaching everyone to guess whatever the crowd will say.

Block 7

A few secret answers keep the whole crowd honest

Agreement has one failure it can never catch by itself. A crowd can slowly drift onto an answer that is comfortable and wrong, and everyone still scores as perfectly coherent while the court hands down a lie. This is ordinary herd behavior, and nothing inside a closed loop of mutual agreement can detect it, because the loop only knows whether people agree, never whether they are right. The check has to come from outside.

So the court does what a wary teacher does with a class it cannot fully trust. It slips in pop quiz questions it already knows the answers to. These calibration cases look identical to live disputes, so no juror can tell which is which, but their answers are fixed in advance, a patch of ground truth in a sea of open questions. They give an absolute grade. A juror who flubs the known cases is weak no matter how neatly they agree with the room, and if the whole pool starts missing them, you are watching capture happen live, early enough to step in. The known answers come from cases that settled without challenge, from verdicts that appeals later confirmed, and from a small expert-checked set at launch. Five to ten percent of the caseload is enough to anchor all the rest to reality.

Why the whole holds

The blocks defend each other

No single block is enough on its own. The strength is that each one covers a hole in another, so every obvious way to cheat the court runs straight into a different part of the design. Read the table as a list of attacks on the left and the piece that blocks each one on the right.

How it could failWhat stops it
A juror grades their own decisive voteleave-one-out scoring
The pool herds onto a comfortable wrong answercalibration anchor + margin weight + vindication
An unlucky or brand-new juror is exiledconfidence gate + rolling window
Lazy voters farm easy coherencerandom assignment + calibration cases
A few veterans dominate every verdictweight cap
An attacker buys their way into the poolearned, maintained reputation
The correct minority is punished for dissentappeal vindication

Stack the blocks together and they quietly change what a smart, selfish juror should do. Coherence pays, and the surest way to agree with six independent peers is to actually be right. Copying the crowd stops working, because it earns almost nothing on close cases and gets caught by the secret quizzes on the rest. Honesty is safe, because the gate only bites when the evidence is overwhelming. Going against the room is worth the risk when you are right, because the appeal can still vindicate you. Every shortcut hits a wall, so the move that pays best turns out to be the one you wanted all along: read the case carefully and vote your honest answer. Game theorists call a setup where no player can do better by switching strategy a Nash equilibrium. This court is rigged so that its equilibrium is the truth.

That is the whole machine. The escalating suspension is its spine, and the confidence gate, the margin weighting, the contrarian credit, and the secret quizzes are the four guardrails that keep a coherence score from hardening into a conformity score. It lets anyone join, keeps jurors anonymous, asks nothing of a webcam or a proctor, and gets more accurate the more the court is used. Its one real dependency is a steady trickle of known answers to grade against, and the court produces those itself every time a case settles cleanly. I would build this.
