The bootstrap

The market that pays the first judges

A self-custody prediction market where every share is a plain token, every answer comes from the court, and the trading fees pay the jurors before a single outside customer arrives.

Market design 7 min read • Act III · Chapter 4 of 7

In one line: a self-custody prediction market whose shares are plain tokens and whose answers come from the court. It works while it is small, it travels in your wallet, and it can be traded in private, and along the way it pays the court's first judges.

Start with an awkward question. How do you pay a judge before anyone has a dispute?

The court from the earlier chapters needs three things to get going, and on day one it has none of them. It needs real questions with real answers, it needs money to pay the people who rule on them, and it needs a stack of settled cases to learn from. A prediction market produces all three as a side effect of people betting, so we build the market first and let it carry the court.

The design borrows the good parts of Polymarket, which proved people will trade on-chain, and changes three things it does the hard way: how a market survives when it is small, where the shares live, and who is allowed to see your bet.

Edge one over Polymarket

Your position should live in your wallet

Polymarket positions are conditional tokens that trade through an order book the company runs off-chain. Your position lives on-chain, but the matching engine and the listing decisions sit with one operator. I want a market where a share is a plain ERC-20 token in your own wallet, tradable on any DEX, usable as collateral in any lending pool, with no operator able to freeze or delist it.

How it resolves

The hard part is not trading. It is deciding who won

Polymarket leans on a token-weighted vote to settle its markets, and a handful of them have ended in public fights where the biggest holders pushed through a result the users disputed. That is the same weakness the court chapter takes apart, so this market hands every resolution to that court instead. Most questions are not controversial. Anyone can post the answer with a bond, and if nobody challenges it inside a short window it simply settles. Only the contested ones convene a jury. There is no outside data feed to trust and nothing to bribe, because the court is the only resolver and it is made of people.

The shape of it

One DAI in, one YES and one NO out

For a binary question, the contract issues two ERC-20 tokens, YES and NO. Deposit one DAI and you mint one YES and one NO. Hand back one of each and you redeem one DAI. A YES and a NO together are always worth one DAI.

Because YES and NO always sum to one DAI, a YES on its own trades somewhere between zero and one, and that price is the crowd's estimate of the probability. Drag the price and watch what the market is saying.

Edge two over Polymarket

It works even while it is small

Put YES and DAI into a standard automated market maker and let people swap. Because the outcome tokens are ordinary ERC-20s, every tool that already exists for ERC-20s works on the first day. A lending market can take YES as collateral. A vault can build a strategy around it. Someone in any country with a wallet can hold it without an account on our site, because there is no account to open.

A new market starts thin, and that is exactly where a Polymarket-style order book struggles, because a quiet book has nobody to trade against. A market maker does not care. It always quotes a price, so the first trader can act on day one. A scoring-rule maker keeps that price sane at any size, a shared collateral pool lets many small markets draw on the same depth, and a router mints fresh YES and NO sets on demand. Thin early liquidity is a starting condition here, not a wall.

When the question resolves, the court reports the result. If it is YES, every YES token redeems for one DAI and every NO token goes to zero. Winners withdraw collateral straight from the contract. Nobody has to trust us to pay, because we never held the money. It sat in the contract the entire time.

Why it cannot be bought

Resolution is where manipulation pays best

If you hold a million dollars of NO, you have a million reasons to force the answer to NO. A token-weighted vote lets you turn that money straight into resolution power, which is why those Polymarket fights happened in the first place. A personhood court cuts the wire. You cannot buy the jury, because the jury is people, drawn at random, voting in private, and the panel is different every time. The cost of bending a result stops tracking the size of your bet.

Edge three over Polymarket

Bets nobody can see coming

Because a share is just a token in your wallet, you can buy it from a fresh address and let the trade blend into ordinary on-chain traffic. A large trader can take a position without telegraphing it to everyone else. That privacy is good for traders, and it turns out to be even better for the court that settles them. Hidden informed money means an attacker cannot tell which markets the smart money cares about, so they cannot guess which cases are worth corrupting or which jurors to lean on. The next chapter is entirely about what these private bets are good for.
