PredictionMoMo

How PredictionMoMo works: betting pools, fixed odds, and ERC-20 tokens

Anyone can open a market on anything by posting a question with their own first bet, even a dime. This chapter covers the whole life of a market: the shared pot that needs no market maker, standing offers at odds you name, graduation into self-custody ERC-20 tokens with an order book, and resolution through bonded assertions and the court.

Prediction markets 10 min read • Act III · Chapter 4 of 10

Two complaints

Two complaints about the markets we love

Post a question, place the first bet, share the link, and that is a whole prediction market. The pot is the money traders draw on, the crowd sets the price, and a court money cannot buy decides who won.

Prediction markets are the closest thing the internet has to a truth machine. Put real money on a question and the number that comes out is the most honest forecast anyone publishes. We are fans. We have exactly two complaints, and this chapter is built around them.

The first is custody: On the big platforms, your position is an entry on an operator's book. The matching engine, the listings, and the freeze button all belong to one company, and your winnings exist at its pleasure. A bet should be a plain token in your own wallet: yours to hold, move, or post as collateral, with nobody to ask.

The second is resolution: Deciding who won is the hard part of every market, and it is the part most designs hand to a committee, an oracle company, or a token vote that a deep enough pocket can lean on. Our answer took two chapters to build: a court money cannot buy. PredictionMoMo exists to be settled by that court, and, as the rest of this act shows, to feed it.

The birth

Born as a shared pot

Here is the entire act of creating a market: You type a question, you bet a dime on the side you believe, and you share the link. That is the whole ceremony. No listing process, no market maker, no liquidity provider, no seed money waiting in a vault.

A new market is a parimutuel pool, the structure Joseph Oller invented for Paris racetracks in 1867 because he wanted betting without a bookmaker setting odds. The pool predates every market maker ever built. Everyone bets into a shared pot, the odds are nothing more than the ratio of the two sides, and when the question resolves the winners split the entire pot pro-rata, minus the 2% fee skimmed at settlement. One refinement is ours: the first bettors set the odds blind, with no crowd to lean on, so their claims carry a time-weight bonus. A dollar staked early earns a larger slice than a dollar that arrived after the picture cleared.

The missing middleman

Why there is no market maker

Ask who loses when a well-informed trader walks into a market. On a quoted market, the quoter does. An automated market maker is a standing offer to trade at a posted price, and whoever knows more than the posting picks it off, trade after trade. That is why quoting engines need a subsidy to survive, and why a subsidized market can never be opened by anyone for pocket change. Someone has to fund the quote before a single bettor shows up.

A pool has nobody to pick off. There is no quote, only a ratio. When sharp money arrives it does not extract from a liquidity provider, because there is none; it simply bets against the other side and moves the odds. A moved number is information, the very thing a prediction market exists to produce. The sharpest trader alive can do nothing to a pool except make it more accurate.

The cost of this design is stated, not hidden. While a market is young there are no locked-in odds and no guaranteed exit before resolution. Your effective odds drift until the pool closes, the way racetrack odds flash until the gate opens, and you cannot sell out of a position early. That is the price of markets anyone can create with zero liquidity, and for the short-dated social questions pools are born for, it costs almost nothing. For markets that outgrow it, the answer is one section down.

No quoter, no bleed, no seed.

Name your odds

Or skip the drift entirely

The pool's drifting odds are the fair price of zero-liquidity birth, and some bettors will not pay it. So from minute one a market also accepts standing offers: name your odds, escrow your side, and wait. Backing NO at 70 cents, up to some size, is not a bet into the ratio. It is a price you have nailed to the wall, borrowed straight from the private room's machinery with a public door on it.

One rule keeps two kinds of liquidity from confusing anyone: the market shows one price and routes through it. The default tap stays the simple pool bet. When a taker bets, the contract first fills any standing offer that beats the pool's current ratio, locked at the offer's posted odds, and overflows whatever remains into the pool. Best execution happens in the contract, so no casual bettor ever does arbitrage homework, and a tiny market with a serious offer suddenly has a serious price: $500 standing at 70 cents says far more about the world than a thirty-cent pool ratio does.

Two lines of small print, both deliberate. Offers earn no early-bettor bonus, because locked odds need no compensation for drift. And filling your own offer moves your governance volume by exactly zero, because the daily-net metric nets a self-trade out of existence before it counts.

Adulthood

Growing up into tokens

An arbitrary volume threshold (like $100,000 total volume) is lazy game theory. A whale could wash-trade both sides of a market just to force it to tokenize, or dump massive capital on a "sure thing" to trigger an order book that opens completely empty.

To prevent manipulation, PredictionMoMo uses a Robustness Strategy. A pool only graduates when it mathematically proves it can sustain an active limit order book by passing three simultaneous checks:

If a whale tries to spoof the maker depth by posting massive standing offers on both sides, they are inadvertently acting as a perfect Market Maker. The time lock ensures their capital sits exposed to the public. If they mispriced their odds, organic bettors will snipe them. If they survive the time lock without getting sniped, they have provided exactly the liquidity the order book needed to launch successfully.

This is where the custody complaint gets its answer. A YES token is not an entry on anyone's book. It is a bearer asset: post it as collateral in a lending pool, move it to any venue that will take it, or hold it through resolution in a wallet only you control. Nobody can freeze the account, because there is no account. Gas is sponsored for verified humans, so holding and moving the tokens never requires pre-funding a wallet with some second currency. If every PredictionMoMo interface vanished tomorrow, the claim in your wallet would still be yours.

Composability

DraftKings parlays, mathematically pure

Bettors love the centralized bookie experience: live fixed odds, concurrent parlays, and early cashouts. But if a decentralized protocol acts as the bookie to quote you a price on a combination of events, it is highly vulnerable to insider knowledge and bankruptcy.

PredictionMoMo achieves all the features of a centralized parlay without the protocol or a "bookie" ever taking risk, using a native integration of the Conditional Tokens Framework (CTF).

By strictly restricting this capability to markets that have already passed the Robustness Lock, we guarantee the underlying tokens are highly liquid. This completely avoids the "ghost town" fragmentation problem that plagues other AMM-based parlay platforms.

The UX problem with manual token splitting

In a standard CTF system, building a parlay is an awful experience. To get a $100 payout on a 2-leg parlay, a user must manually deposit $100 to mint 4 exhaustive combinatorial tokens (YES/YES, YES/NO, NO/YES, NO/NO), keep the one they want, and beg the order book to buy the other 3. If there are no buyers, the user gets stuck holding "junk" tokens they never wanted.

PredictionMoMo solves this with Atomic Routing (Fill-or-Kill). The user simply clicks "Buy Parlay" on a clean bet slip. Under the hood, a single smart contract transaction flash-borrows the money from the order book, mints the tokens, and sells the unwanted ones instantly. If there aren't enough buyers on the order book, the transaction mathematically reverts. You never get stuck holding tokens you don't want.

By leveraging atomic routing, PredictionMoMo acts as an incorruptible escrow rather than a casino. The Live Odds of the parlay are simply the current bids on the limit order book for the combinatorial token. To Cash Out Early, bettors do not ask the house for a buyout; they simply dump their token into the open order book to the highest bidder.

The private room

Markets behind an invitation

Everything so far has been public: a pool anyone can join, a book anyone can trade. The fourth feature is a room. A private, invite-only market where you set the odds yourself and they never move.

It is a handshake bet with the escrow built in. Post your side at any odds you name, say $700 on YES at 70 cents, and send the link to whoever you want on the other side. Accepting means depositing the matching $300 of NO, and from that moment the odds are locked at exactly what you posted. No crowd, no drift. More invitees can join either side at the same fixed price for as long as the two sides stay matched, and unmatched money walks home at close. Resolution is the machinery you already know: an asserted outcome, a challenge window, the court for anything contested.

Two things make the room more than a convenience. It is protocol escrow in its simplest clothes: two parties, a disagreement with money locked in the middle, and an arbiter already attached, which is the exact shape of the deals apps will route here later. And a settled private market gives the court something no public market can: a real question with a verified answer that never appeared on any public page. With the participants' consent it becomes a blind test for jurors, one that cannot be looked up, and the loop chapter puts those tests to work.

Resolution

How a market ends

The event happens: Now somebody has to say so, and this is where most markets quietly hand the job back to a trusted operator. Here the job is open to anyone, and it runs in three moves.

  1. 1

    Anyone asserts the outcome

    Whoever saw the result posts it along with a bond. No oracle company, no resolution committee, no waiting for an operator to wake up. The bond is the assertion's skin in the game.

  2. 2

    A one-week window, watched for profit

    A false assertion forfeits its bond to whoever catches it, so watching the windows is a paid job, not a civic hobby. The night shift is staffed by bounty hunters, and a lie asserted into a sleeping market is a bounty on the liar's own head. A full week of window means the lie has to survive every timezone awake, day after day, and nobody is left waiting on it: on tokenized markets the YES and NO tokens keep trading straight through the window, so anyone who wants out sells, and only the final conversion to dollars waits.

  3. 3

    Payout, or the court

    If the window closes quietly, the pool pays out pro-rata and the market is history. If someone challenges, the locked pot crosses into the court lane, and the final verdict releases it by rule.

Roughly 95% of markets end at step two: one assertion, one quiet window, payout. The funnel below is the whole resolution story at a glance.

The contested few cross into the court: a panel of verified humans, drawn by a public beacon, voting in encrypted ballots, with a dispute ladder above them for the truly stubborn. The money is already inside protocol escrow, so the verdict is not advice; it is the release rule. And the quiet settlements are not wasted. Every one that passes curation becomes an answer key that grades the jurors, which is the engine the loop chapter is about.

The economics

The fee, printed once

The whole business model is one number, printed once. A 2% fee comes off every pot at settlement and splits three ways: 70% pays the jurors, 20% feeds the reward pool that pays the active, high-quality ones, 10% runs the protocol. The 2% base and its split are constitutional and route first; PredictionMoMo's own token may vote a margin of up to one extra point on top, which is the company's revenue, and the juror payroll never depends on it. PredictionMoMo pays for the court that settles it, and the court is what makes it worth betting in.

That is PredictionMoMo: born for a dime, priced by its own crowd, grown into tokens you hold yourself, and ended by a bonded assertion or a court nobody can buy.
