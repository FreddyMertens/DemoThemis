PredictionMoMo

How PredictionMoMo works: betting pools, fixed odds, and ERC-20 tokens

This chapter follows the six PredictionMoMo promises: arbitration whales cannot buy, self-custody YES and NO tokens, private markets with neutral escrow, instant market creation from the first bet, tradeable claims during disputes, and peer-to-peer parlays without bookie risk.

PredictionMoMo: markets anyone can open

  1. Arbitration not gameable by UMA whales: no chance of getting scammed.
  2. Liquid markets tokenize into ERC-20 YES and NO tokens you self-custody; this lets you provide liquidity and earn interest, post them as collateral, and trade them with leverage.
  3. Anyone can open private, invite-only markets and still have a world-class third-party escrow and dispute system: post your side at any odds you name, invite the other side, and the odds stay fixed for everyone who joins.
  4. Start a new prediction market instantly, on any topic, with any liquidity, even with less than a dollar.
  5. With self-custody tokens that stay tradeable during disputes and resolution windows, you can exit the market at any time if there is liquidity. This also means there is no rush to close dispute windows, allowing retroactive changes to resolution windows where necessary.
  6. Native peer-to-peer parlays on liquid markets. By integrating Conditional Tokens Framework (CTF) via token splitting, bettors get live odds and early cashouts without the protocol or a "bookie" ever taking bankruptcy risk.

Value prop 1

1. Arbitration not gameable by whales

Prediction markets do not fail when traders disagree. They fail when everyone has to stop trading the event and start trading whoever gets to define the event. UMA showed one version plainly: token-weighted truth turns a disputed outcome into a governance market with a whale chart attached.

The point is not that every disputed Polymarket resolution was corrupt. Some were genuinely ambiguous. The point is worse: the resolution layer made whale weight, voter conflicts, and after-the-fact rule fights part of the price discovery. Four episodes explain why PredictionMoMo cannot inherit UMA's court.

Ukraine mineral deal clearest whale case

Reporting traced a disputed Yes resolution to one UMA whale voting through three accounts with about five million UMA, roughly a quarter of the vote. That is the cleanest example of token weight pushing the arbiter itself.

The Defiant Market

Voters with positions systemic evidence

WSJ and Barron's reported recurring overlap between UMA voting power and Polymarket exposure, including large-wallet concentration and voters resolving markets they could also bet on.

Zelenskyy suit public scandal

Photos and major-media descriptions were not enough to stop a No resolution after the UMA dispute path. The word "suit" left room for argument, but the public lesson was brutal: common-sense evidence still had to survive a token vote.

WIRED Coindesk Market

Strategy Bitcoin sale plain reality lost

Strategy disclosed that it sold Bitcoin during the market window, yet the corresponding Polymarket contract resolved No after the dispute process. This is the best example of a correct event prediction losing to resolution mechanics.

Galaxy Research Coindesk Market

Kalshi's Khamenei carveout platform as judge

Kalshi shows the centralized version of the same failure: the house can write the escape hatch and then judge it. Plaintiffs alleged Kalshi used its Khamenei death carveout to avoid full Yes payouts and settle at the last traded price. The venue's incentive is self-protection: limit legal risk, precedent risk, and ugly death-market fallout. The user's incentive is simpler: pay the event as written. DemoThemis removes that conflict. Rules are fixed before escrow, and edge cases go to randomly drawn, conflict-screened jurors. Ambiguity remains, but the venue no longer rules on its own book.

ClassAction.org The Verge Market

The conclusion is simple: a prediction market needs an arbiter neither whales nor the venue itself can own. UMA shows the whale failure: token weight becomes adjudication weight. Kalshi's Khamenei dispute shows the house failure: the venue can write the exception and then judge the exception. PredictionMoMo's answer is not a nicer token vote or a more trusted operator. It removes both from the final verdict.

Easy markets still clear through bonded assertions, but contested markets go to verified humans, randomly drawn, voting privately, with one person counted once. That is the service upgrade: a market where the price can move freely, but the court cannot be bought by the biggest wallet or rewritten by the house.

How a market ends without a bought judge

The event happens: Now somebody has to say so, and this is where most markets quietly hand the job back to a trusted operator. Here the job is open to anyone, and it runs in three moves.

  1. 1

    Anyone asserts the outcome

    Whoever saw the result posts it along with a bond. No oracle company, no resolution committee, no waiting for an operator to wake up. The bond is the assertion's skin in the game.

  2. 2

    A one-week window, watched for profit

    A false assertion forfeits its bond to whoever catches it, so watching the windows is a paid job, not a civic hobby. The night shift is staffed by bounty hunters, and a lie asserted into a sleeping market is a bounty on the liar's own head. A full week of window means the lie has to survive every timezone awake, day after day. On tokenized markets, section 5 explains why that longer window does not freeze traders.

  3. 3

    Payout, or the court

    If the window closes quietly, the pool pays out pro-rata and the market is history. If someone challenges, the locked pot crosses into the court lane, and the final verdict releases it by rule.

Roughly 95% of markets end at step two: one assertion, one quiet window, payout. The funnel below is the whole resolution story at a glance.

The contested few cross into the court: a panel of verified humans, drawn by a public beacon, voting in encrypted ballots, with a dispute ladder above them for the truly stubborn. The money is already inside protocol escrow, so the verdict is not advice; it is the release rule. And the quiet settlements are not wasted. Every one that passes curation becomes an answer key that grades the jurors, which is the engine the loop chapter is about.

Value prop 2

2. Self-custody tokens for liquid markets

Tokenization is when a pool becomes a real market asset. Your claim turns into plain ERC-20 YES and NO tokens you can hold in your wallet, lend, borrow against, route through other venues, or combine into more complex bets. The question is when a pool has earned that power. Section 5 comes back to the resolution benefit: because this claim is transferable, final settlement can take the time it needs without trapping every holder.

An arbitrary volume threshold, like $100,000 total volume, is lazy game theory. A whale could wash-trade both sides of a market just to force it to tokenize, or dump massive capital on a "sure thing" to trigger an order book that opens completely empty.

To prevent manipulation, the market uses a Robustness Strategy. A pool only graduates when it mathematically proves it can sustain an active limit order book by passing three simultaneous checks:

This is where the custody complaint gets its answer. A YES token is not an entry on anyone's book. It is a bearer asset: post it as collateral in a lending pool, move it to any venue that will take it, or hold it through resolution in a wallet only you control. Nobody can freeze the account, because there is no account. Gas is sponsored for verified humans, so holding and moving the tokens never requires pre-funding a wallet with some second currency. If every PredictionMoMo interface vanished tomorrow, the claim in your wallet would still be yours.

Value prop 3

3. Private markets with neutral escrow

This is the feature existing prediction markets mostly skip: a private, invite-only market with real third-party escrow. Set the odds yourself, share the link, and those odds never move.

It is a handshake bet with the escrow built in. Post your side at any odds you name, say $700 on YES at 70 cents, and send the link to whoever you want on the other side. Accepting means depositing the matching $300 of NO, and from that moment the odds are locked at exactly what you posted. No crowd, no drift. More invitees can join either side at the same fixed price for as long as the two sides stay matched, and unmatched money walks home at close. Resolution is the machinery you already know: an asserted outcome, a challenge window, the court for anything contested.

Two things make the room more than a convenience. It is protocol escrow in its simplest clothes: two parties, a disagreement with money locked in the middle, and an arbiter already attached, which is the exact shape of the deals apps will route here later. And a settled private market gives the court something no public market can: a real question with a verified answer that never appeared on any public page. With the participants' consent it becomes a blind test for jurors, one that cannot be looked up, and the loop chapter puts those tests to work.

Value prop 4

4. Start a market with the first bet

Here is the entire act of creating a market: You type a question, you bet a dime on the side you believe, and you share the link. That is the whole ceremony. No listing process, no market maker, no liquidity provider, no seed money waiting in a vault.

A new market is a parimutuel pool, the structure Joseph Oller invented for Paris racetracks in 1867 because he wanted betting without a bookmaker setting odds. The pool predates every market maker ever built. Everyone bets into a shared pot, the odds are nothing more than the ratio of the two sides, and when the question resolves the winners split the entire pot pro-rata, minus the 2% fee skimmed at settlement. One refinement is ours: the first bettors set the odds blind, with no crowd to lean on, so their claims carry a time-weight bonus. A dollar staked early earns a larger slice than a dollar that arrived after the picture cleared.

Value prop 4

4.1 Why tiny markets need no market maker

Ask who loses when a well-informed trader walks into a market. On a quoted market, the quoter does. An automated market maker is a standing offer to trade at a posted price, and whoever knows more than the posting picks it off, trade after trade. That is why quoting engines need a subsidy to survive, and why a subsidized market can never be opened by anyone for pocket change. Someone has to fund the quote before a single bettor shows up.

A pool has nobody to pick off. There is no quote, only a ratio. When sharp money arrives it does not extract from a liquidity provider, because there is none; it simply bets against the other side and moves the odds. A moved number is information, the very thing a prediction market exists to produce. The sharpest trader alive can do nothing to a pool except make it more accurate.

The cost of this design is stated, not hidden. While a market is young there are no locked-in odds and no guaranteed exit before resolution. Your effective odds drift until the pool closes, the way racetrack odds flash until the gate opens, and you cannot sell out of a position early. That is the price of markets anyone can create with zero liquidity, and for the short-dated social questions pools are born for, it costs almost nothing. For markets that outgrow it, the answer is one section down.

No quoter, no bleed, no seed.

Value prop 4

4.2 Fixed odds when the pool price is not enough

The pool's drifting odds are the fair price of zero-liquidity birth, and some bettors will not pay it. So from minute one a market also accepts standing offers: name your odds, escrow your side, and wait. Backing NO at 70 cents, up to some size, is not a bet into the ratio. It is a price you have nailed to the wall, borrowed straight from the private room's machinery with a public door on it.

One rule keeps two kinds of liquidity from confusing anyone: the market shows one price and routes through it. The default tap stays the simple pool bet. When a taker bets, the contract first fills any standing offer that beats the pool's current ratio, locked at the offer's posted odds, and overflows whatever remains into the pool. Best execution happens in the contract, so no casual bettor ever does arbitrage homework, and a tiny market with a serious offer suddenly has a serious price: $500 standing at 70 cents says far more about the world than a thirty-cent pool ratio does.

Two lines of small print, both deliberate. Offers earn no early-bettor bonus, because locked odds need no compensation for drift. And filling your own offer moves your governance volume by exactly zero, because the daily-net metric nets a self-trade out of existence before it counts.

Value prop 5

5. Markets keep trading while resolution runs

This is where the token layer from section 2 pays off. If a market has tokenized, YES and NO stay transferable through assertion windows, challenges, and court review. Anyone who wants out can sell to someone willing to hold resolution risk; final redemption waits, but price discovery does not.

That means the court does not have to rush. Longer challenge windows and retroactive fixes become possible because users are not trapped inside a frozen platform balance while the answer is checked.

Value prop 6

6. Peer-to-peer parlays without bookie risk

Bettors want live fixed odds, parlays, and early cashouts. The dangerous version is a house quoting combinations from its own balance sheet, where insider knowledge and bankruptcy risk sit in the middle of every bet.

The parlay layer gives that familiar experience without making the protocol a bookie, using a native integration of the Conditional Tokens Framework (CTF).

By strictly restricting this capability to markets that have already passed the Robustness Lock, we guarantee the underlying tokens are highly liquid. This completely avoids the "ghost town" fragmentation problem that plagues other AMM-based parlay platforms.

The UX problem with manual token splitting

In a standard CTF system, building a parlay is an awful experience. To get a $100 payout on a 2-leg parlay, a user must manually deposit $100 to mint 4 exhaustive combinatorial tokens (YES/YES, YES/NO, NO/YES, NO/NO), keep the one they want, and beg the order book to buy the other 3. If there are no buyers, the user gets stuck holding "junk" tokens they never wanted.

The interface solves this with Atomic Routing (Fill-or-Kill). The user simply clicks "Buy Parlay" on a clean bet slip. Under the hood, a single smart contract transaction flash-borrows the money from the order book, mints the tokens, and sells the unwanted ones instantly. If there aren't enough buyers on the order book, the transaction mathematically reverts. You never get stuck holding tokens you don't want.

By leveraging atomic routing, the contracts act as market escrow rather than a casino. The Live Odds of the parlay are simply the current bids on the limit order book for the combinatorial token. To Cash Out Early, bettors do not ask the house for a buyout; they simply dump their token into the open order book to the highest bidder.

The economics

The fee that pays the court

The whole business model is one number, printed once. A 2% fee comes off every pot at settlement and splits three ways: 70% pays the jurors, 20% feeds the reward pool that pays the active, high-quality ones, 10% runs the protocol. The 2% base and its split are constitutional and route first; the market's own token may vote a margin of up to one extra point on top, which is the company's revenue, and the juror payroll never depends on it. The market pays for the court that settles it, and the court is what makes it worth betting in.
