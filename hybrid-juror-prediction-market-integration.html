The bootstrap loop

How PredictionMoMo gives DemoThemis cases, fees, answer keys, and proof

A new arbitration market has no cases, no money, and no track record. PredictionMoMo breaks that loop by creating paid resolution work from day one, then turning settled markets into the evidence that lets DemoThemis win outside disputes.

Bootstrap loop 12 min read • Chapter 5 of 8

The trap

The cold start arbitration cannot dodge

A court is only trusted once it has a record, but nobody pays for an untested court. PredictionMoMo breaks that loop by handing DemoThemis paid cases, fee revenue, and public proof before outside apps have to take the first risk.

The trap is circular: no track record means no paying cases, no paying cases means no money for jurors, and no real jurors making real calls means no track record. Subsidies can paper over one corner for a while, but a court propped up by a grant is racing a clock it set for itself.

The way in

The first paying customer is a bet

PredictionMoMo manufactures disputes people already want settled. Someone opens a market on an election, a match, a price, or a private bet. To pay out, the market has to decide what actually happened, and that decision is arbitration with money already locked inside it.

That gives DemoThemis three things at once. Markets need answers, so the court has cases. Traders pay fees, so jurors can be paid from use. Objective markets settle against reality, so the court gets graded answer keys instead of asking the world to trust an empty badge.

The money

Fees pay the judges, not a printing press

The pay for jurors comes from use. A 2% fee is skimmed from every pool at settlement and split once: 70% to juror pay, 20% to the reward pool that pays active, high-quality jurors, 10% to operations. The court breathes with traffic: more demand raises pay pressure, more jurors spread it out, and careless rulings slash the live reserve into the reward pool.

The answer keys

Settled markets keep the court tied to reality

The hybrid system grades each juror by leave-one-out agreement with their panel, which is the right everyday ruler. But agreement alone can drift. If the room learns the same wrong habit, everyone still agrees.

Objective markets give the court an outside audit. A final score, a price at a fixed minute, an election result: these are questions reality can grade. When a market settles cleanly, it leaves behind a genuine question, argued over by people with stakes, with the world's answer attached.

Clean keys

Watched windows and curation keep the library clean

A false market assertion forfeits its bond to whoever catches it. That turns challenge windows from civic duty into paid work: watchers cover thin markets because catching a lie is profitable, and would-be liars fund the patrol.

The toggle is the whole argument. With the rule on, the defaults show near-full coverage and a zero in the last box, and notice who funds the patrol: the would-be liars themselves, one forfeited bond at a time. The design never asks anyone to be vigilant. It makes vigilance a business.

Even a watched settlement is not automatically an exam question. It has to pass curation: enough volume, a clean window, real watcher coverage, and a spot-check that the question meant what it said. Public keys audit the juror pool. Private-room keys, admitted only by participant consent and never shown to their own participants, audit individual jurors because they cannot be looked up.

Stare at the hand-written column for a second. A court that writes its own exams can keep roughly ten jurors honestly rated, forever, because editors do not scale. A court fed by markets keeps forty rated at modest volume, four hundred at ten times that, and the supply climbs on its own every day betting does. The thing that keeps jurors honest is the one input the platform manufactures faster the more it is used.

The climb

Three phases, each standing on the last

PredictionMoMo is the first rung, not the destination. The record it builds is what lets DemoThemis step out into the wider world of disputes.

  1. 1

    Markets only

    DemoThemis settles PredictionMoMo markets first. Fees pay jurors, settled markets grade them, and a public history of fair calls starts to pile up.

  2. 2

    Open the doors to real disputes

    With a record in hand, the protocol starts taking outside escrowed deals: small on-chain work, creator payouts, marketplace refunds, and app-specific rubrics where funds are already locked and a release rule is needed.

  3. 3

    Become the default

    Once enough apps route funds through the same protocol escrow, signed verdict receipts and measured court accuracy become proof new integrations can verify instead of rebuilding a support desk from scratch.

Why it compounds

One wheel, not three repeated stories

Now stand back and watch it turn. More markets create more fees and answer keys. More fees pay jurors. More answer keys calibrate them. Better verdicts make markets and outside escrow safer. That safety brings more demand.

PredictionMoMo + DemoThemis

Bets become paid cases

The market needs resolution anyway, so its ordinary operation gives the court real work before outside customers arrive.

Fees + reserves

Use funds the bench

Settlement fees pay jurors, and bad rulings slash reserves into the reward pool that pays active, high-quality ones.

Personhood + markets

Growth incentives reach humans

Fee rebates, airdrops, and early-market incentives can go to real people instead of bot farms.

Apps + escrow

Proof makes integrations easier

Outside apps can inspect a live record of verdict accuracy and route funds through protocol escrow instead of trusting a promise.

A subsidy is a countdown. A prediction market is an engine: it creates disputes, pays for their resolution, grades the people resolving them, and publishes the proof that brings the next customer in.
