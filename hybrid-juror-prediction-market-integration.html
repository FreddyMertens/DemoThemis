The loop

How markets train the court: settled bets become juror answer keys

Every market that settles on hard fact leaves behind a question with a verified answer. This chapter shows how those settlements are watched, curated into an answer-key library, and used to keep thousands of jurors measurably honest, so the court's calibration grows automatically with betting volume.

The flywheel 10 min read • Act III · Chapter 5 of 9

The problem

The one rope to reality

To stay honest, the juror court needs a steady drip of questions with known answers. A prediction market makes them by the thousand, almost for free, as a side effect of people betting. Here is how the two click together.

A school that has lost its answer sheets can still hand out grades. Mark every student against the class average. The marks look precise. They are also untethered, because if the whole class learns the same wrong fact, every exam confirms it.

The court runs the same risk. The hybrid system grades each juror by leave-one-out agreement with their panel, and that is the right everyday ruler, because on most disputes nobody holds a certificate of the truth. But agreement is a relative measurement. It can tell you a juror moved with the room. It cannot tell you the room is still pointed at the world. A pool that slowly absorbed a fashionable error would keep agreeing with itself, scores green, verdicts drifting.

So the check has to come from outside the scoring, from questions the jurors did not write and cannot vote on. Where do you find thousands of real questions whose answers arrive from the world itself, on a schedule? You are standing on them. Every market on the platform is a question with money riding on it, and the great majority are questions reality will grade personally: a final score, a price at a fixed minute, an election called by morning. When such a market settles, what it leaves behind is exactly the artifact the court is starving for. A genuine question, argued over by people with stakes, with the world's own answer stapled to it and a timestamp on the staple.

Agreement keeps its job. It is the everyday ruler, cheap and always available. The keys are the audit that tells you the ruler has not bent.

Nobody is paid to write these exams. They are minted as ordinary exhaust of people betting on things they care about.

One stream of these, flowing daily, is the rope that ties the court's scores to the world. The next two sections are about keeping that rope clean, because a grading instrument is only worth what its worst entry is.

Quality control

Watched windows make clean keys

An answer key is only as trustworthy as the settlement that minted it, so look hard at how a market settles. When the event lands, anyone may assert the outcome by posting a bond, and a one-week challenge window opens. If nobody objects, the assertion stands, the pool pays out, and no juror is disturbed. Roughly 95% of markets end exactly this way, quietly, which is what keeps the court small and the fees at 2%.

The obvious worry: who is awake at three in the morning, watching the window on a thin market about second-division volleyball? If watching costs more than it pays, a rational watcher stays in bed, and a lie asserted into an empty room sails through.

One rule removes the dilemma. A false assertion forfeits its bond to whoever catches it. Not to a burn address, not to the protocol, to the catcher. The challenge window stops being a civic duty and becomes a bounty board, and watching the long tail of thin markets becomes a paid job that bots are happy to work around the clock. The toggle is the whole argument. With the rule on, the defaults show near-full coverage and a zero in the last box, and notice who funds the patrol: the would-be liars themselves, one forfeited bond at a time. The design never asks anyone to be vigilant. It makes vigilance a business.

The window is a deliberately unhurried full day. Tokenized positions keep trading while it runs, so nobody is actually waiting, and a lie has to survive every timezone awake instead of one quiet stretch.

The filter

Curated, not collected

Even a cleanly watched settlement is not automatically an exam question. Before a settled market is allowed to grade anyone, it passes through curation, and curation is picky on purpose. A market becomes an answer key only if it carried enough volume for real attention to have been on it, its challenge window ran while watcher coverage was actually live, and it survives a spot-check. Everything else still pays out normally. It just never gets to mark a juror's record.

Settled private rooms join the library the same way, by participant consent, and curation prizes them above everything else, because a question that never had a public page is the cleanest test there is. One rule rides along: a juror who was inside the room is never handed its key.

That ordering is the safety. Suppose a lie does slip through some unwatched corner, the rare case the last widget let you build. The bettors on that one market are hurt, and that loss is real. But the lie cannot poison the court, because the library of answer keys is chosen, not automatic, and a thin market with no watchers is precisely the kind the filter throws out. The failure stays local. The instrument stays clean.

Assumes a juror needs about two fresh keys a day to hold a confident rating across their rolling 50-to-150 case window, and that a human editorial team can write and verify about twenty keys a day, a ceiling near ten jurors no matter the budget.

Stare at the hand-written column for a second. A court that writes its own exams can keep roughly ten jurors honestly rated, forever, because editors do not scale. A court fed by markets keeps forty rated at modest volume, four hundred at ten times that, and the supply climbs on its own every day betting does. The thing that keeps jurors honest is the one input the platform manufactures faster the more it is used.

Here is the full path one market walks from pocket change to the library.

  1. 1

    Born for a dime

    Someone posts a question about Sunday's final and backs it with their own first bet. The link travels, the pot grows, and the market crosses the volume threshold that says real attention is on it.

  2. 2

    Reality answers

    The final whistle blows. Someone asserts the outcome with a bond, and the one-week challenge window opens with that bond hanging over it.

  3. 3

    The window runs watched

    Watchers are staking out the window because catching a lie pays the liar's bond. No challenge lands. The pool pays out pro-rata, the 2% fee is skimmed, and the settlement is logged with its coverage intact.

  4. 4

    Curation says yes

    Enough volume, a clean watched window, and a spot-check that the question meant what it said. The market enters the answer-key library. Most settlements stop one step short, and that is the filter working.

  5. 5

    The key starts grading

    Folded into juror scores beside the leave-one-out marks, the key becomes one more public test the pool either passes or fails. The bettors got their payout days ago. The court keeps the question forever.

Five steps, no editor, no commission, no budget line. The library writes itself and the filter signs off.

Honest limits

What the keys can and cannot measure

Be precise about what this instrument reads, because it reads one thing superbly and another not at all.

What it measures well is the pool. Curated keys are folded into juror scores alongside the leave-one-out agreement marks, which gives the court a measurement no amount of internal coherence could produce: how often its panels land on answers reality later confirmed. If panel verdicts start diverging from the keys across the whole pool, that is drift, caught early and with confidence, the exact failure that agreement-only grading is structurally blind to. Hundreds of keys a day make the test statistically sharp within weeks.

What a public key cannot do is trap an individual. A public settlement can be looked up, so a lazy juror who quietly checks one scores correct anyway. Two things close that gap. The first is incentives that bite case by case: scoring credited only above the obvious-vote baseline, reputation driving draw rate and per-case pay, and the flat five-dollar bond sized at the laziness floor, all built in the hybrid system chapter. The second is a sharper kind of key: the settled private room. Its question and its outcome never appeared on any public page, so when it lands in a juror's docket there is nothing to look up, and anyone who was inside the room is never handed it. Public keys audit the pool. Private keys audit the person.

The flywheel

The wheel

Now stand back and watch it turn. More betting settles more markets, and the curated cut becomes more answer keys. More keys hold more jurors to a confident rating, which raises the floor of every panel drawn. Sharper panels return verdicts the next bettor can rely on, and reliable settlement is the one feature a betting venue cannot fake its way to. So, more betting.

The money turns with it. The same settlements that mint the keys pay the 2% fee, and 70% of that fee is juror pay, so the wheel that sharpens the jurors is also the wheel that feeds them.

And notice what the wheel throws off as it spins: a record. Every curated key is a public test the court either passed or failed, timestamped, free for anyone to recount. Most referees ask to be trusted. This one publishes its own grading, continuously, at the speed of settlement.

That record is the product. When an escrow contract, a marketplace, or an ad network decides whether to route its disputes here, the thing it points at is that running public measurement of accuracy, thousands of graded calls and counting. How the court spends that proof to win its first outside customers is the next chapter.
