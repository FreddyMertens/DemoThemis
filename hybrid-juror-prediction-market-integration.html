Why it compounds

The build sheet

The court that feeds itself

The juror court needed a steady drip of known answers to stay honest. A prediction market makes them by the thousand, almost for free, as a side effect of people betting. Here is how the two click together.

Prediction markets 11 min read • Act III · Chapter 5 of 7

Here is the one weakness the juror court could never fix from the inside.

The hybrid juror court grades its jurors on how well they agree with their peers. To stop a whole crowd from quietly agreeing on something false, it slips in cases whose answer is already known and watches who gets them right. Those known answers are the court's one rope tied to reality. The trouble is that someone has to keep supplying the rope, and a court working alone runs short fast. Answer keys written by hand are slow to make and quick to run out.

So pair the court with a machine that prints known answers as ordinary exhaust. A prediction market is that machine. People bet on questions the world will settle on its own, and the moment it does, the closed market turns into a graded quiz the court can use. Bolt the two together and each one quietly feeds the other.

Start here

A price is just a crowd's probability

Strip away the jargon first. A prediction market is a question with a live number attached. Take something with a yes-or-no answer, like will it rain tomorrow, and sell a contract that pays one dollar if the answer turns out yes and nothing if it turns out no. Whatever that contract trades at is the crowd's probability. A contract changing hands at 62 cents is a crowd saying it is about 62 percent sure, and the number twitches the instant anyone learns something new. Prices quietly soak up scattered private knowledge, which is the grain of truth inside the efficient-market hypothesis.

For there to always be someone on the other side of your bet, whoever opens a market puts up the first pot of money and hands it to an automated market maker, a small program that will always quote a price. The neatest design, the market scoring rule from economist Robin Hanson, rests on a proper scoring rule that pays out most when its stated odds are honest, so the maker never runs dry and its running price reads like a real forecast. Anyone can open a market on anything, and the only ticket in is the money they seed it with.

Who decides

Most questions settle without a fight

Now the lucky part. The great majority of things people bet on have a hard answer waiting in the future. A final score, the price of a coin at a fixed minute, who won an election, last month's rainfall. When the moment comes, anyone can post that answer to the market with a bond, which opens a short challenge window. If the answer is obvious, nobody bothers to dispute it, the window closes, and the market pays out on its own. The court has settled the case without a single juror lifting a finger.

That cheap path is the same optimistic layer the standalone court runs on, and here it does double duty. An answer that closes unchallenged is, by definition, one everyone agreed was plainly true, which makes it a clean known answer the court can keep for later. Only a thin slice ever get challenged. Those, and only those, reach a jury. The jury is the referee of last resort, not the everyday scorekeeper.

The referee

When the court does step in

For the cases that reach it, nothing about the original design changes. A random panel of seven jurors is drawn, weighted toward those with the strongest records. Each one locks in a sealed vote and only then reveals it, a commit and reveal step so nobody can copy a neighbour. Each juror is then graded by leave-one-out coherence, which checks their vote against the others' verdict and never against a tally they helped build. A near-tie barely moves anyone's standing, a lopsided result moves it a lot, and a bonded appeal can escalate the case to a larger panel that pays back a lone juror who turns out to have been right.

The payoff

Every settled market is a free answer key

This is where the loop earns its keep. In the standalone court, the secret quizzes had to be written and checked by hand, which made them scarce and slow. On the market they appear on their own. The instant a market resolves against hard ground truth, an election called or a price confirmed at the closing bell, it turns into a question whose answer is now known for certain. The platform flags it quietly, slips it back into a juror's caseload looking like any other case, and checks whether the juror got it right. The juror cannot tell the graded quiz from a live dispute, which is exactly the point.

The supply scales with use rather than with a budget. A court hearing a hundred cases a day mints a thick stream of fresh keys from the markets that closed objectively, while a court writing its own quizzes would still be waiting on its editors. Spend about a tenth of each juror's caseload on these known answers and the whole crowd stays pinned to reality.

The twist

Why the anonymous bets matter most

Plenty of bettors would rather not be watched. Someone with a strong, well-researched view often does not want to broadcast it, because showing your hand moves the price before you have finished buying. So the platform lets bets be placed privately, through zero-knowledge orders that prove a bet is valid without revealing who placed it or how large it is. The privacy is not a frill. It draws in the well-informed money that makes a market's eventual answer worth trusting, and it does so without that money tipping its hand to everyone else.

For the court, those bets are close to ideal fuel. They pour informed signal into the events that later become answer keys, and because they never show up in the public flow, no juror or attacker can guess which resolved markets the court will lean on hardest. The blinding the original design worked hard for comes for free. To a juror, a high-stakes private market that just settled looks like any other case on the docket.

The real prize is that the answer keys grow with the platform instead of drying up. Drag the dial and watch the court's calibration supply, and the number of jurors it can keep honest, move with the volume.

One case, start to finish

Follow a single market all the way through

Take a real-looking example. Someone opens a market on whether a candidate will win an election, and watch one trip around the loop.

  1. 1

    The creator seeds the market with 50,000 in stablecoins and trading begins. Public bets and quiet private ones flow in, and the price drifts to 62 cents, the crowd's live read on a yes.

  2. 2

    Election night arrives and the result is plain. Someone posts the winning answer with a bond, nobody bothers to challenge it, and when the window closes the market pays out on its own. No jury is needed, because the answer was never in doubt.

  3. 3

    The outcome is now hard fact, and this market drew heavy informed money. So the platform secretly tags it as an answer key, the kind of known case the court uses to stay honest.

  4. 4

    That answer key is dropped into jurors' caseloads dressed as an ordinary dispute. None of them can tell it apart from a live one, which keeps the test fair.

  5. 5

    Each juror who ruled on it earns a plain right-or-wrong mark, on top of their usual leave-one-out agreement score, both scaled by how lopsided the verdict was.

  6. 6

    Those marks feed each juror's reputation, a beta distribution kept over a rolling window with an error bar around it. Jurors who called it right drift up. One who was confidently wrong watches their lower bound slide toward the suspension line.

  7. 7

    Reputation then decides who gets drawn next and how loudly their vote counts, up to a cap. Sharper jurors are picked more often, verdicts get better, and more people show up to bet. The loop has come all the way around.

Under the hood

What it takes to build

None of this needs exotic parts. On-chain, a factory contract spins up each market with its market maker, and a router settles every market through the optimistic layer, escalating only the challenged ones to the contract that draws the panel and records the votes. Private bets live in a shielded pool that accepts zero-knowledge deposits and reports only the aggregate volume, never the identities behind it.

Off-chain sits the reputation engine. It keeps each juror's beta distribution and rolling window, watches resolutions for ground truth, decides which settled markets become answer keys, and folds the results back into every score. One guard rail matters more than the rest. Every juror is a verified unique person through proof of personhood like World ID, so nobody can conjure a thousand fake jurors in a Sybil attack and outvote the honest ones.

Jurors stay anonymous to each other and to traders. Bettors stay anonymous to the public. Only the reputation engine, behind the curtain, ever ties a juror to a vote to an outcome, and only to keep the score.

The flywheel

Why the loop speeds up

Notice what the loop does to everyone's incentives. A juror's best move is simply to be right, since reputation, and with it the odds of being drawn and the weight of their vote, climbs with accuracy on both real cases and the hidden answer keys. Traders get verdicts they can lean on. The people who open markets and supply liquidity earn fees when those markets settle cleanly, and the protocol takes a cut that pays for the arbitration layer. Everyone wins by making the wheel turn faster.

This is a network effect aimed at accuracy rather than mere size. The court that once had to ration its known answers now has more than it can use, and the surplus grows every day the market stays open.

The whole idea

A court that grows its own answer keys

Strip it to one sentence. The juror court was good at judging but hungry for known answers, and a prediction market produces known answers as fast as people are willing to bet. Wire the two together and the court's one scarce ingredient becomes its most abundant one.

The three rules the original design stood on all survive. Judge jurors by real judging, move against nobody until the evidence is sure, and anchor the crowd to answers you already hold. The only thing that changes is where those answers come from. They no longer have to be written by hand. They arrive on their own, every time someone bets on a question the world will eventually settle.
