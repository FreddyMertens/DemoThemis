The economics

Follow the dollar

The money story fits in one picture because the design refuses to have two of anything. One fee, skimmed from every pool at settlement. One printed split. The invoice routes first by constitution, the PredictionMoMo token keeps only the margin voted above it, and no token anywhere prints the juror payroll. Jurors are paid from use, the reward pool that pays active, high-quality jurors is fed by the fee and by slashed bonds, and the protocol's share keeps the lights on.

Zero to one

The first week, by the numbers

Cold starts die at the first fee. So the launch sequence follows one rule: the initial conditions must cost the first participants nothing. Here is what the first week actually looks like.

  1. 1

    The first juror pays nothing

    Entry starts with low capacity instead of a fixed price. A new juror's retained fees build the live reserve automatically, and that reserve grows only as the seat carries more unresolved work. You earn capacity before you expand it. The pool starts wide because nothing is in the way, and width is the security.

  2. 2

    The first market costs a dime

    Creating a market is posting a question with your own opening bet, any size. The pot is the liquidity, the odds are the ratio of the sides, and there is no maker anywhere to subsidize or to bleed. Early bettors earn a time bonus for setting odds blind.

  3. 3

    The first settlement pays a watcher

    The first false assertion forfeits its bond to whoever catches it, which makes watching the windows a job rather than a charity from day one. Honest settlements flow into the curated answer-key library that will grade jurors from week two onward.

  4. 4

    The first dispute prices itself

    No admin sets the appeal bond. The three floors compute it from the live case: fund the next panel, beat the re-roll, pay the delay. A near-tie verdict discounts the climb, and the loser-side petition splits the bill among the sincerely aggrieved.

  5. 5

    The first cheater funds the honest jurors

    Reports never expire. When the correlation audit catches a colluding clique, their slashed bonds and banned World IDs feed the reward pool that pays the honest jurors, without reopening a single settled market.

Privacy boundary

What the protocol learns

The identity layer proves one thing and withholds the rest. World ID and the on-device face check say a unique, enrolled human is present for this draw, on this attested device, inside this grace window. DemoThemis does not receive an iris scan, a face image, a face template, a legal name, or a reusable person handle. It receives a purpose-scoped proof tied to the case and the seat commitment, useful here and nowhere else.

The rest of the machine keeps the same split. The draw is public because anyone must be able to check the beacon. The ballot is private because only the tally should ever open. Reputation and scoring update from verifiable case records, curated answer keys, and appeal outcomes, not from a private operator's discretion. Answer-key curation has to stay permissionless and challengeable. No component should be able to join personhood, seat, vote, score, and payout history into one table; the architecture goal is not to guard that table, but to avoid creating it.

Still owed

The two debts we publish

A blueprint that hides its open items is a brochure. There are two, and both are scheduled rather than hoped for. The locked box has no centralized fallback: DemoThemis will not run arbitration through an admin server, a multisig, or a single tally coordinator. The production requirement is keyless or distributed setup, where no party ever holds a usable ballot-opening secret, any initialization artifact is unusable afterward, and every tally ships with a proof anyone can check. If a design requires trusting one operator to hold a key, delete a key, pick panels, rewrite scores, or admit answer keys by discretion, it is not this design.

And the court for subjective disputes, the kind reality never grades, ships later, narrow, and under its own name. It opens with rubric-checkable cases only, grades its jurors on rubric coherence, split prediction, and appeal survival, and never borrows the objective court's track record, because that record measures a different skill. The routing rule is one bit wide: will reality post the answer? Everything else follows from it.
