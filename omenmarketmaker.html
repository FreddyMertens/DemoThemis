Value prop 1

1. Bullet proof arbitration that whales cannot buy

Prediction markets do not fail when traders disagree. They fail when everyone has to stop trading the event and start trading whoever gets to define the event. UMA showed one version plainly: token-weighted truth turns a disputed outcome into a governance market with a whale chart attached.

Even an ambiguous dispute exposes the structural failure: whale weight, voter conflicts, and after-the-fact rule fights become part of price discovery. Four episodes show why OmenMarketMaker uses independent, unbuyable arbitration instead of a token-weighted court.

The conclusion: a prediction market needs an unbuyable arbiter. UMA shows the whale failure; Kalshi's Khamenei dispute shows the house failure. OmenMarketMaker removes both from direct control of the final verdict.

Every deposit contributes proportionally to a locked resolution reserve. The work-based jury target and the maximum contribution percentage are published before betting starts. A funded market pays DemoThemis automatically and receives a ruling from randomly drawn verified humans; an underfunded market draws no jury and returns its liquidity instead.

How a market ends with an unbuyable judge

The event happens: OmenMarketMaker stops new betting under the written close rule, keeps the outcome collateral locked, and begins one consistent five-step resolution path.

  1. 1

    The market closes under fixed rules

    The question, permitted answers, evidence rules, and close time were locked before trading. No operator can rewrite the case after seeing the money or likely outcome.

  2. 2

    The reserve is checked automatically

    Each deposit has contributed at the fixed percentage. If the locked reserve covers the jury quote, DemoThemis is paid automatically. Any excess reserve is returned proportionally.

  3. 3

    An underfunded market unwinds

    If the reserve is short, the creator gets one fixed top-up window. Without a complete top-up, no jury is drawn: YES and NO positions receive their deposited backing at their recorded prices, and the market closes without a fee.

  4. 4

    Verified humans decide a funded case

    DemoThemis locks the case before a public draw, seats verified humans, aggregates private ballots, and runs the appeal ladder when a valid appeal is funded.

  5. 5

    The signed ruling returns

    OmenMarketMaker verifies the final DemoThemis ruling, applies the market's written release rule, redeems claims, routes its fee, and closes the record.

The service boundary stays deliberately narrow: only a funded case and its locked resolution payment enter DemoThemis; a signed ruling comes back. Outcome collateral, the market fee, and settlement remain with OmenMarketMaker.

Every sufficiently funded market uses the court: verified humans drawn by a public beacon, voting in encrypted ballots, with an appeal ladder above them when the stakes justify another panel. The signed ruling selects the pre-written release rule. A market that cannot cover the locked jury price never reaches that draw; after its top-up window it automatically refunds YES and NO backing at the positions' recorded prices. Every completed court case contributes to the shared juror-quality record, and independently checkable outcomes can add later confirmation. The loop chapter explains how that evidence compounds.

Value prop 2

2. Start a market with the first bet

Here is the entire act of creating a market: You type a question, you bet a dime on the side you believe, and you share the link. That is the whole ceremony. No listing process, no market maker, no liquidity provider, no seed money waiting in a vault.

A new market is a parimutuel pool, the structure Joseph Oller invented for Paris racetracks in 1867 because he wanted betting without a bookmaker setting odds. Everyone bets into a shared pot and the odds are the ratio of the two sides. Every deposit supplies the same pre-declared percentage to the locked resolution reserve. The jury target is a funding gate, not a collection cap: if the reserve covers the target at close, DemoThemis receives the actual jury payment, every YES and NO position receives its proportional share of any reserve surplus, and winners split the remaining outcome backing after OmenMarketMaker's 2% market fee. One refinement is ours: early claims carry a time-weight bonus.

Value prop 2

2.1 Why tiny markets need no market maker

Ask who loses when a well-informed trader walks into a market. On a quoted market, the quoter does. An automated market maker is a standing offer to trade at a posted price, and whoever knows more than the posting picks it off, trade after trade. That is why quoting engines need a subsidy to survive, and why a subsidized market can never be opened by anyone for pocket change. Someone has to fund the quote before a single bettor shows up.

A pool has nobody to pick off. There is no quote, only a ratio. When sharp money arrives it does not extract from a liquidity provider, because there is none; it simply bets against the other side and moves the odds. A moved number is information, the very thing a prediction market exists to produce. The sharpest trader alive can do nothing to a pool except make it more accurate.

Young markets begin as self-balancing pools, so the first stake can open a real market with almost no capital. The moving ratio turns every new bet into better price discovery. As volume and depth grow, the market graduates into fixed offers, self-custodied tokens, order-book liquidity, and early exits.

No quoter, no bleed, no seed.

Value prop 2

2.2 Fixed odds when the pool price is not enough

The pool's drifting odds are the fair price of zero-liquidity birth, and some bettors will not pay it. So from minute one a market also accepts standing offers: name your odds, escrow your side, and wait. Backing NO at 70 cents, up to some size, is not a bet into the ratio. It is a price you have nailed to the wall, borrowed straight from the private room's machinery with a public door on it.

One rule keeps two kinds of liquidity from confusing anyone: the market shows one price and routes through it. The default tap stays the simple pool bet. When a taker bets, the contract first fills any standing offer that beats the pool's current ratio, locked at the offer's posted odds, and overflows whatever remains into the pool. Best execution happens in the contract, so no casual bettor ever does arbitrage homework, and a tiny market with a serious offer suddenly has a serious price: $500 standing at 70 cents says far more about the world than a thirty-cent pool ratio does.

Two lines of small print, both deliberate. Offers earn no early-bettor bonus, because locked odds need no compensation for drift. And filling your own offer moves your governance volume by exactly zero, because the daily-net metric nets a self-trade out of existence before it counts.

Value prop 3

3. Self-custody tokens for liquid markets

Tokenization is when a pool becomes a real market asset. Your claim turns into plain ERC-20 YES and NO tokens you can hold in your wallet, lend, borrow against, route through other venues, or combine into more complex bets. The question is when a pool has earned that power. Section 4 comes back to the resolution benefit: because this claim is transferable, final settlement can take the time it needs without trapping every holder.

An arbitrary volume threshold, like $100,000 total volume, is lazy game theory. A whale could wash-trade both sides of a market just to force it to tokenize, or dump massive capital on a "sure thing" to trigger an order book that opens completely empty.

To prevent manipulation and preserve a simple refund path, the market uses a Robustness Strategy. A pool only graduates when it can sustain an active limit order book and has fully funded resolution by passing four simultaneous checks:

If a whale tries to spoof the maker depth by posting massive standing offers on both sides, they are inadvertently acting as a perfect Market Maker. The time lock ensures their capital sits exposed to the public. If they mispriced their odds, organic bettors will snipe them. If they survive the time lock without getting sniped, they have provided exactly the liquidity the order book needed to launch successfully.

This is where the custody complaint gets its answer. A YES token is not an entry on anyone's book. It is a bearer asset: post it as collateral in a lending pool, move it to any venue that will take it, or hold it through resolution in a wallet only you control. Nobody can freeze the account, because there is no account. Gas is sponsored for verified humans, so holding and moving the tokens never requires pre-funding a wallet with some second currency. If every OmenMarketMaker interface vanished tomorrow, the claim in your wallet would still be yours.

Value prop 4

4. Markets keep trading while resolution runs

This is where the token layer described in section 3 pays off. If a market has tokenized, YES and NO stay transferable while the verified jury and any funded appeals run. Anyone who wants out can sell to someone willing to hold resolution risk; final redemption waits, but price discovery does not.

That means the court does not have to rush. Careful jury and appeal windows remain practical because users are not trapped inside a frozen platform balance while the answer is decided.

Value prop 5

5. Bookmaker UX without a central bookmaker

Parlays use only tokenized markets that have passed the Robustness Lock. The customer requests one fixed quote; permissionless solvers compete to price the complete combination, and a quote can execute only after its maximum payout is locked on-chain.

There is no house setting every price or carrying an unsecured promise. Protected lenders earn borrowing interest while keeping their original outcome exposure; separate junior capital knowingly earns the parlay spread and bears the correlation risk.

Value prop 6

6. Private markets with neutral escrow

This is the feature existing prediction markets mostly skip: a private, invite-only market with real third-party escrow. Set the odds yourself, share the link, and those odds never move.

It is a handshake bet with the escrow built in. Post your side at any odds you name, say $700 on YES at 70 cents, and send the link to whoever you want on the other side. Accepting means depositing the matching $300 of NO, and from that moment the odds are locked. Each deposit contributes proportionally to the room's pre-declared resolution reserve. A fully funded reserve pays DemoThemis automatically; if it remains short after the creator's top-up window, no jury is drawn and both sides receive their backing at the locked YES/NO prices.

Two things make the room more than a convenience. It is protocol escrow in its simplest clothes: two parties, a disagreement with money locked in the middle, and an arbiter already attached, which is the exact shape of the deals apps will route here later. And a settled private market gives the court something no public market can: a real question with a verified answer that never appeared on any public page. With the participants' consent it becomes a blind test for jurors, one that cannot be looked up, and the loop chapter puts those tests to work.

The economics

Independent products, automatic resolution funding

Before the first bet, a market fixes its work-based jury target and reserve contribution percentage, bounded by the disclosed maximum. Every deposit bears that same percentage, even after the target is reached. At close, OmenMarketMaker automatically transfers only the actual jury payment to DemoThemis before any jury is drawn; excess reserve returns to every YES and NO position proportionally, regardless of which outcome wins. OmenMarketMaker's separately disclosed 2% settlement fee still belongs to its own economics.
