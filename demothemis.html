4 min overview

The simple version

DemoThemis is a bullet proof, unbuyable court for any well-defined case. Anyone can fund the jurors and receive a ruling; when money is involved, optional protocol escrow can execute it automatically.

Problem and role

Why a neutral court matters

Why it exists

Online trust already depends on arbiters

1

You trust the backstop

When you book, buy, ride, rent, sell, or bet online, you are trusting whoever can reverse the payment if the deal goes wrong.

2

The backstop is usually conflicted

A platform, bank, venue, or token vote can be slow, expensive, house-owned, or buyable by the largest holders.

3

Escrow changes the power

When assets are at stake, protocol escrow lets the app provide the interface without controlling the funds or outcome.

4

A shared court can compound

Many apps can use one arbiter, and the court gets better as more cases create fees, records, and evidence of juror quality.

Case lifecycle

What happens from filing to final ruling

  1. Case and fee lock

    The requester submits the question, evidence rules, and juror fee before anyone is drawn. If assets are involved, they may also enter protocol escrow.

  2. Jurors are drawn

    World-ID humans enter the pool. A public randomness beacon draws one panel, bound to the case. Anyone who does not assess themselves as capable of judging it can decline, and the next standby takes the seat.

  3. Presence is checked

    A draw-bound face check confirms the enrolled human is present now, without giving DemoThemis a face database.

  4. Votes stay private

    Receipt-free encrypted ballots let the court count the result without revealing or proving any single vote.

  5. Appeals widen

    A loser can climb to a larger panel by paying a bond or gathering a real loser-side petition.

  6. The ruling returns

    DemoThemis returns a final signed ruling. Connected escrow, if any, releases or refunds by rule; collusion audits can continue afterward.

DemoThemis locks the full work-based quote before anyone is drawn, so the selected panel is fully funded before the case begins.

Attack resistance

What makes the court unbuyable

Attack model

Each lock removes a different shortcut

1

One juror = one human

Money can buy tokens, but it cannot mint more unique people. A verdict has to be assembled from humans, not wallets.

2

Careless voting has a cost

Jurors can be privately penalized for careless mistakes, making guessing unprofitable without giving richer people more voting power.

3

The jury cannot be rerolled

Each case gets one publicly verifiable random draw. The requester, an app, and DemoThemis must accept that panel; they cannot discard it and try again.

4

Rented accounts get squeezed

A per-draw face check makes a one-time credential hard to operate remotely at the moment a vote matters.

5

Paid votes cannot prove delivery

A briber pays only for a vote they can verify. Receipt-free ballots make that proof unavailable even to a willing seller.

6

Big cases need more than one lucky panel

Panel sizes grow with stakes, and the largest cases can require several panels to agree at once.

Appeals and finality

An appeal brings in a larger, fresh jury

A losing party can fund a new decision by a larger panel: 7 jurors, then 15, then 31. When no appeal is funded—or the final panel rules—the decision becomes final. Later proof of collusion can punish jurors, but it cannot reopen the ruling or a completed settlement.

Problem and role

Why a neutral court matters

You never really trusted the stranger whose spare room you booked. You trusted the arbiter standing behind the deal. Make that arbiter unbuyable and affordable, and more apps can rely on it.

Booking a room from a stranger and paying before arrival looks reckless on paper. It feels ordinary because a platform promises to refund you, a card issuer can reverse the charge, and reviews punish repeat cheating. The safety comes from the arbiter behind the stranger.

Every marketplace, gig app, ad auction, and app store rents this backstop from a platform, bank, or token vote. The result can be house-owned, slow, expensive, or buyable by the largest holders. DemoThemis turns that hidden utility into shared, unbuyable arbitration, with optional protocol escrow when assets need automatic settlement.

One court, many apps

Build the arbiter once, share it everywhere

Any person or app can submit a well-defined case, fund its jurors, and receive an independent ruling from randomly drawn, verified humans. If assets are involved, optional escrow can execute that ruling automatically.

The requester supplies the case, decision criteria, evidence rules, and juror fee. DemoThemis returns the ruling; when assets are involved, optional escrow can release or refund them by rule. Each customer replaces a private backstop with one shared court.

Why shared history compounds

The history is useful without being public

Each case leaves only the history needed to grade juror quality and prove the court followed its rules: who served, how the panel split, whether an appeal changed the result, and whether reality later confirmed it. When escrow is attached, the same receipt proves it followed the ruling. This is not a public reputation feed. Parties and the requester receive a signed receipt, while scoring receives the minimum case data it needs. Public markets resolve publicly because traders need settlement; private arbitrations stay private by default.

Attack resistance

What makes the court unbuyable

The bullet proof claim comes from every lock working together: verified humans, random panels, live reserves, private ballots, and panels that grow with the stakes.

The wrong foundation

When votes are tokens, verdicts have a price

Token courts sell the gavel to the deepest pocket. This court seats verified humans instead: a live reserve priced by workload, a die nobody can re-roll, a face check that proves a real person is voting, and a ballot that cannot prove how it voted. Buying it means buying people, one at a time, in the dark.

In a token court, voting weight follows the wallet, so a majority has a market price. Disputed Polymarket resolutions show the problem in practice; the evidence is in the OmenMarketMaker chapter.

That is not merely a bad implementation. If stake is voting power, the protocol works as written when the largest holder wins. An attacker reads the rule as a price list: acquire enough tokens and buy the verdict.

DemoThemis swaps the foundation to one human, one vote. Every seat is backed by a World ID that no wallet can mint twice or exchange can sell in bulk. An attacker must recruit people separately, keep them present, land them in a sealed draw, and somehow enforce their private votes. Every funded case that reaches DemoThemis faces every lock below at once.

The seat

The self-pricing seat

Picking a fixed reserve that merely looks painful is a guess, and guesses age badly as fees, workload, and the juror pool change.

The reserve has one job: make coin-flipping lose more to penalties than it earns in fees. Bribery belongs to the encrypted ballot; rented seats belong to the face check. The court therefore derives the minimum reserve from pay per vote, the extra error caused by carelessness, and the number of unresolved votes still at risk. More work raises it; more active jurors spread the work and lower it.

Verified humans opt in without buying a governance token or posting the reserve upfront: the protocol fronts the live reserve and sponsors the required gas. The juror's private account still receives any final debit, so free entry does not make careless service free.

In plain terms, the floor is the income exposed while outcomes are pending divided by the expected penalty from the extra mistakes carelessness creates. The inputs are observable court traffic, not a governance guess, so the seat reprices itself as conditions change.

A reserve above that live floor makes the court less safe. It evicts honest jurors and thins the pool while paid recruits stay, increasing their share. The widget shows why the best reserve sits just above the laziness floor, not at an impressive fixed number.

Discipline sits in the penalty, not the headline reserve: a wrong-side vote loses a larger slice after a lopsided final verdict and a smaller one after a near tie. The quote

Price the work, not a permanent split

Before the draw, DemoThemis locks one transparent quote: processing cost + required panel compensation + reward-reserve top-up + capped operations charge.

1

Pay follows the work

Juror pay responds to eligible supply, case volume, panel size, expected time, and complexity.

2

The reserve fills to target

Cases add more below the published coverage target and nothing once the reserve is full.

3

Operations has a ceiling

An approved, public budget is recovered only up to a hard per-case cap, never as a permanent revenue share.

4

Each rung pays for itself

Each initial quote funds its required panel; every appeal bond funds its own larger panel and delay.

Pricing inputs move only between published epochs. Governance may set the jury-fee ceiling only within the lab's 0.5–3% range; the cost quote can be lower. The complexity class and quote freeze before selection, and any case that exceeds the ceiling must show an explicit minimum or subsidy instead of cutting juror pay.

The draw

The sealed die

If an app can change a case ID, trigger time, cancellation point, or replacement rule after inferring the panel, it can grind draws until a small bloc wins. Even a one-percent bloc becomes almost certain to succeed if it may discard enough unfriendly panels.

The protocol instead binds one funded, committed case to one value from a public randomness beacon: a service that publishes fresh, unpredictable numbers on a fixed schedule that no requester or court operator can steer. The juror fee and case contents lock before the panel exists, and anyone can reproduce the draw afterward. A selected seat whose wallet holds the disputed position is skipped by deterministic replacement. A juror who does not assess themselves as capable of judging the case may also decline, and the next deterministic standby takes the seat. The requester gets one roll, not a panel-shopping interface.

The face check

The same person, every time

A one-time iris scan cannot stop someone renting out their phone or login later. A hundred rented credentials controlled by one operator would look like a hundred independent humans and vote like one.

When a seat is drawn, World App therefore performs an on-device face match on an attested phone; only a pass unlocks the ballot. An annual re-scan provides a slower backstop by retiring dead and abandoned credentials, but only the per-draw check closes the rental window for the case itself.

The privacy boundary is strict: DemoThemis never receives the scan or face template. It accepts only a short-lived, draw-bound proof that the World-ID-backed seat is present for this case now—not a name, face, reusable login, or global identity handle.

Presence proves the enrolled juror is there; the private ballot then prevents anyone paying them from verifying how they voted.

The ballot

The ballot nobody can sell

A vote buyer pays only when they can prove delivery. Commit-and-reveal fails because the juror publishes a vote hash and later opens it: the same receipt that proves honesty to the protocol proves obedience to a briber.

DemoThemis instead locks every ballot under a threshold scheme and lets the juror silently replace it without any public sign. An encrypted NO receipt proves only what was submitted once, not whether the counted ballot later became YES. The briber cannot know which valid submission was final.

Ballots are then added while they remain encrypted. Only the aggregate total opens, with a zero-knowledge proof that the count followed the rules; no individual ballot opens, even to tally operators. This is stronger than hiding a public vote behind an administrator. It removes the individual result anyone could sell.

Under commit-reveal the receipt is proof and the trade clears. Under threshold voting with re-keying, the same receipt proves nothing, because you may have re-keyed one minute later, and no briber pays for maybe.

The diagram shows the deliberate boundary. Drawn seats must be public enough for anyone to reproduce the panel, but each case uses a new pseudonym and receiving key. The public can verify selection without joining one person's appearances into a voting history.

Silent ballot replacement means that even a disclosed seat and a complete transcript cannot prove what ultimately counted.

No administrator key is allowed. The tally is acceptable only if no operator ever receives a usable ballot-opening key. Setup must be distributed or keyless, so independent participants create the capability together without one party holding it; initialization artifacts become unusable afterward; and the sole output is an aggregate tally with a public zero-knowledge proof. A party that creates, holds, or merely promises to delete a complete key breaks the guarantee.

Privacy continues after tallying. Exact scores, reserves, rewards, penalties, and refunds stay inside private commitments—sealed account records whose rules can still be proved. Zero-knowledge proofs reveal only what is needed now, such as enough reserve, an eligible reputation band, or a valid private update, while batched totals prove the money balances. A public payment or penalty tied to a seat would recreate the receipt.

The panel

Panels that grow with the pot

A $40 listing dispute and a $200,000 escrow should not buy the same protection. Panels therefore scale with stakes: 7 seats for small cases, 15 for serious ones, and 31 for the largest, with appeals climbing the same rungs.

A private proof of long service gates 31-seat draws. Seniority costs an honest juror nothing but takes an attacker years to fake, the useful asymmetry a financial barrier cannot provide. Money buys more strangers, never more voice for one person.

The ceiling

What happens above the biggest panel

A 31-seat ceiling would leave a $5 million and $50 million case protected by the same jury while the attack prize keeps growing. Simply making one jury bigger is unsafe: it amplifies whichever side already dominates the pool, reporting a tilted pool's answer with greater confidence. Pool width, not one enormous sample, is the real protection.

Above a value line, DemoThemis instead draws several non-overlapping 31-seat panels at once and requires all of them to agree. Any disagreement raises an alarm rather than printing a verdict.

An attacker must now win every independent panel on the same sealed roll. If one panel falls one time in five, three together fall only one time in 125. Adding panels as the pot grows restores the rising takeover price.

Juror quality

How careful jurors stand out

Every completed case contributes evidence about juror accuracy without turning the first majority into the answer key.

Across many cases, DemoThemis combines useful agreement above the obvious, consistency with written criteria, appeal survival, and later independent evidence when available. Careful jurors are drawn and paid more while careless ones receive less.

The grader's paradox

Agreement is the wrong thing to pay for

Grade jurors on matching the majority and the safe strategy becomes guessing the room instead of reading the case. The diagram shows how a wrong consensus can produce perfect-looking scores.

This is not a rare edge case. Once agreement determines careers, herding is the stable strategy: independent judgment adds risk but no reward. The design instead scores useful judgment above the obvious majority, checks whether decisions fit the case's written criteria and survive appeal, and adds later independent evidence when available. The loop chapter explains how completed cases strengthen the record.

The scoring rule

Credit above the obvious

Every case a juror touches is scored by leave-one-out agreement with the rest of the panel. The rule has three moves, and each one shuts a door the herd would otherwise walk through.

  1. 1

    Leave one out

    Remove your vote and compare it with the others, so your ballot cannot prop up its own grade. On a 31-seat panel, the reference is the other 30.

  2. 2

    Subtract the obvious

    Ask what someone who never opened the file could guess. Matching a 29-to-2 landslide earns roughly nothing; credit starts above that baseline.

  3. 3

    Weight by margin

    A 16-to-15 split was hard, so landing on the right side earns more. Weight scales down toward a landslide: how often are you right when being right was difficult?

Two jurors can have the same agreement rate and completely different scores.

Every completed case updates the same quality record. Difficulty-adjusted agreement, consistency with the written criteria, and appeal survival provide ongoing evidence; later independent outcomes add an external check when available. The complete rule is useful judgment above the obvious, tested against every reliable signal the case can provide.

The reputation dial

A score with an error bar attached

Per-case credits form a private reputation commitment. Mathematically it is a beta distribution: a best estimate plus an honest error bar, not one naked percentage. An 80% record over 12 cases is therefore not treated like 80% over 140.

The system carries a 95% interval over a rolling window of the latest 50 to 150 cases. Neither the exact score nor its case history is published; a juror proves only the band or threshold needed for the current action. The table shows what that private dial may change.

The three-times draw cap matters because court security comes from a wide active pool, not a small class of stars. Accuracy still compounds into more work and private income, but no juror dominates selection.

Lenient on purpose

Why the gate waits so long

A hair-trigger suspension rule would reward safe majority guesses. If one rough stretch could end a career, rational jurors would avoid hard positions and follow the apparent room. Suspension therefore fires only when the full interval is below 0.70, meaning the evidence is confidently and persistently poor.

Leniency at that last gate is not softness everywhere. Private pay, draw rate, vote weight, and margin-scaled reserve penalties respond earlier; only their aggregate transfer is public. Low effort becomes unprofitable before uncertain evidence ends a career.

Wrong-side updates use the post-appeal outcome, not the first panel's verdict. If a 31-seat appeal overturns a 15-seat panel, the original dissenter's private debit and score update are reversed, while the overturned majority takes the loss. No public refund identifies the dissenter. Near-tie penalties were already small; vindicated dissent costs nothing.

Disputes

The ladder, and the two clocks

An appeal redraws at the next size, 7 to 15 to 31. Its bond is the highest of three live floors: pay the next panel, make re-rolling unprofitable (capture odds times the stake), and compensate for delay. Near ties receive a discount, but never below those floors.

A loser can post the bond alone or gather at least 5% of losing-side verified humans, each staking a refundable share. This lets one convinced dissenter climb without letting thousands of sore losers appeal with costless clicks. Failed appeals fund the reward pool; vindicated appellants receive a private refund and bonus.

Each rung uses one published 2–8 week window. If nobody funds the next rung—or the 31-seat rung ends—the ruling becomes final and returns to the requester; attached escrow, if any, settles. Later collusion can change private bonds and eligibility, not the final ruling or completed settlement.

The appeal bond

Why a wider pool makes appeals cheaper

The capture floor is probability times stake, not the whole stake. A ten-million-dollar case does not automatically require a ten-million-dollar bond. In a wide pool, a small bloc almost never wins a panel majority, so multiplying the prize by that tiny probability can drive the capture floor below the simple cost of seating the next panel.

As stakes grow, panels expand from 7 to 15 to 31 and capture odds can collapse faster than the prize rises. On a healthy pool, an appeal therefore falls back toward juror pay plus delay compensation. Security and affordability improve together.

One court

Different evidence, one quality record

Every case uses the same random draw, private ballots, appeal ladder, and juror-quality system. The question and decision criteria are fixed before the draw, and every final case contributes evidence about juror accuracy.

A market result may later provide independent confirmation. A delivery dispute can show whether jurors applied a written specification consistently. A community-standard case contributes difficulty-adjusted agreement and appeal evidence. The available signals vary; whether the case counts does not.

Scores combine useful agreement above the obvious majority, consistency with the written criteria, appeal survival, and later independent evidence when available. No case type is excluded, and no first majority becomes automatic truth.
