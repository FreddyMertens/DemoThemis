Design response · round four

What deserved to die?

This round you killed two of my designs and caught one of my arguments misfiring. Good. This page buries the dead honestly, rebuilds on what you proposed instead, and answers the four questions you asked straight.

Repairs, round four 17 min read • Deep dive · answering How deep do the fixes go?

A design review where the designer wins every round is not a review. It is a performance. This round I lost twice, and both losses made the system better, which is the whole point of doing this in public.

The ladder pricing you accepted, so it ships as written and gets no further ink. Everything else moved. You rejected the market-set seat deposit for a reason I should have weighed harder, the cold start. You corrected my reading of your appeal petition, and the corrected version teaches something neither of us had on the page yet. You asked whether the cryptography is real or a research wish, whether the face check survives contact with actual users, and what I would concretely do about the court that grades the ungradable. And you vetoed the market-maker plan with a requirement that, once I sat with it, pointed at a mechanism a Paris racetrack solved in 1867.

The route. First the seat reserve stops being a number and starts pricing itself from workload. Then your petition gate, rebuilt around who actually signs. Then a parts-bin audit: what is on a shelf today versus what would be an invention. Then the face check, graded for gameability. Then markets that are born with a dime. Last, my recommendation for the subjective court, stated as a decision rather than a survey. Two of my earlier designs die on this page, and I will say so plainly when each one goes.

Your verdict 01 · the seat bond

How does the reserve price itself?

You rejected the bonded-tier market, and your reason was not a preference. It was arithmetic I had been carrying since round one and failed to apply. The capture widgets all said the same thing: the security parameter of this court is the width of the juror pool. A bloc that owns 1% of a huge pool almost never lands a majority; the same bloc in a thin pool owns the place. Anything that thins the pool, including a clever deposit market, sells the load-bearing wall to buy furniture. And the cold start makes it worse, because the pool is thinnest exactly when the court most needs it wide. So the tiered deposit dies here. You were right.

What replaces it has to answer one question honestly: what was the reserve actually for? In round two I gave it three jobs, and the later rounds quietly took two of them away. Deterring bribery moved to the locked box, because a bribe you cannot verify is a bribe you do not pay. Blocking rental moved to the face check and the annual re-scan. Look at what is left and the reserve has exactly one job remaining: make careless voting unprofitable. A juror who flips a coin instead of reading the case must lose more from slashes than they collect in fees. That is the whole job.

Let's put numbers on the game mechanics: A careless juror differs from a careful one in one measurable way: they are wrong more often. The court measures the income a juror can earn while their votes are still unresolved, then divides it by the extra wrong-rate from carelessness. That gives the live reserve floor. When demand rises faster than active jurors, each juror carries more income at risk and the reserve rises. When more jurors join, the work spreads out and the reserve falls. No fixed dollar amount survives contact with traffic.

Illustrative. Juror turnout falls as the reserve rises; the attacker's recruits do not, so their share of a shrinking pool grows. Slide the reserve right and watch security get worse as the entry burden gets bigger. The sweet spot sits just above the laziness floor and moves with workload.

The widget makes the counterintuitive part visible: past the floor, raising the reserve reduces security. The slashable stake per juror grows linearly while the pool, and with it the capture math, collapses much faster. A wide court with light reserves is safer than a small court with heavy reserves against every attack we have modeled, and it is not close.

Two boundaries keep this clean: The live reserve is the juror's seat reserve only; the appeal bonds on the dispute ladder stay stake-scaled, because those are posted by disputants who by definition have stake-sized money in the fight, and pricing them never thins the juror pool. And big cases still get hardened panels, just not with money: stakes scale the panel from 7 to 15 to 31, and the highest panels draw only from jurors with longer case histories. Seniority is the one gate that costs a juror nothing to pass and an attacker years to fake.

The design I would ship

A live reserve at the laziness floor, and zero friction on day one your call, adopted

Every seat carries a live reserve set at the laziness floor, computed from current pay pressure and unresolved workload. Entry on day one costs nothing at all: new jurors start with low capacity, and their first retained fees build the reserve automatically until the seat can carry more work. You earn capacity before you expand it. The deposit market from round three is dead, and the underwriter with it; a self-pricing reserve needs no financier. What survives is the discipline, the slash on wrong-side votes, now sized to police effort rather than to impress attackers, because impressing attackers was never the reserve's job once the other locks existed.

Your verdict 02 · the appeal petition

The gate only the losers can summon

You called my game theory wrong, and on the central point you are correct. My round-three diagram had the error's beneficiaries outvoting its victim, but your rule never gave winners a vote. Nobody signs a petition against their own payout; signing is dominated for every winner. The petition is a head-count of the losing side, the only side motivated to dispute, which is what you said and what I argued past. So let me throw away my strawman and analyze the rule you actually proposed, because the correct analysis finds something better than my objection, a selectivity problem you can see on one screen.

Ask who can clear a fixed threshold of verified losing-side signers. In a big market the losing side is hundreds or thousands of verified humans, and every one of them just lost money, which is all the motivation signing requires. Being aggrieved is free and universal among losers, so a flat threshold of twenty five is cleared by essentially every big market that resolves, right or wrong. Meanwhile the thin 3am market where an asserter just stole the pot might contain three losers, or one. The flat gate cannot be met there at any threshold above the number of victims. Put those together and the gate's selectivity runs exactly backwards: it waves through the abundant, low-value disputes and blocks the rare, load-bearing one.

Illustrative. With the flat threshold, drag the market size up and down and watch the two cards disagree with justice in both directions. The repair ties the threshold to the losing side's own size, with a small refundable stake per signer so mass signing is priced rather than free.

The repair stays inside your framework, which is what makes it satisfying. Keep the petition, keep the verified-losers-only electorate, but make the threshold a fraction of the losing side rather than a constant, with a floor of one, and attach a small refundable stake to each signature. Now the thin market's lone watchdog clears the gate alone by staking, the big market's thousand sore losers can still appeal but each signature costs something that is only refunded if the appeal wins, and the petition becomes what you wanted it to be all along, a Sybil-resistant measure of sincere aggrievement rather than a free re-roll coupon. It composes with the OR-gate from round two unchanged: petition or bond, either opens the door, and the dispute ladder above the door is what keeps grinding priced.

Where this lands

Your gate, with the threshold made proportional corrected and adopted

I withdraw the beneficiaries-outvote-victims argument; it answered a rule you did not propose. The argument that survives is about gate selectivity, and the proportional threshold with staked signatures fixes it while keeping every property you wanted: verified humans only, motivated signers only, collective cost-sharing, and a hard filter in front of the panel ladder. This is the version I would write into the spec, with your name on the idea and the percentage left as a parameter to tune against live dispute data.

Your question 03 · is the cryptography real?

Off the shelf, or off the map?

Fair challenge, because the locked box does sound like a research paper. The honest answer is that every component is running in production somewhere today, none of it is a breakthrough, and none of it gets a centralized fallback. Assembly is required. Invention is not.

The one honest asterisk is the setup, not an operator. Stock MACI often describes a coordinator, but DemoThemis cannot depend on a party who can peek and promise not to. The launch requirement is stricter: no usable ballot-opening key is ever held by one actor, and no emergency admin key exists. The setup must be distributed, keyless, or burned into an unusable state before any case can rely on it, with the aggregate tally and its zero-knowledge proof as the only thing the public needs to trust. If that cannot be met, the locked box does not launch.

Your question 04 · the face check

What a face can and cannot prove

Three questions, three straight answers. Implementable today: yes. The check is the same on-device face match that unlocks a phone, and World App already ships it as Face Auth for sensitive actions, so for a juror the flow is a push notification, a glance, and a ballot, two or three seconds of friction. The grace window and standby seats from round three absorb the juror who was asleep, so smooth UX is a solved layout problem, not a research one.

How gameable: gameable at the top, not at the bottom. A printed photo or a video on a second phone fails modern liveness detection, which reads depth and reflection and micro-movement. The serious attack is a rooted device injecting a deepfake straight into the camera feed, and the defense is hardware attestation, the phone proving its sensor stack is untampered, which World App already leans on. That raises the attack from cheap to specialist. For the highest tier, add one more rung: random mid-case re-checks, and an Orb re-verification requirement for seats sitting on 31-seat panels, expensive checks reserved for expensive cases.

The ladder tops out at presence. No sensor can prove the person voting is thinking for themselves, which is why independence is bought with the live reserve and the unverifiable bribe, never proven with hardware.

So the recommendation stands with its limits stated: the face check is the only per-vote, person-present primitive deployable today, the alternatives check the device or the credential or the past, and the one thing no check reaches, the willing accomplice who votes as instructed, is already handled by the locked box making the instruction unenforceable. Sensors prove presence. Incentives buy independence. Asking either to do the other's job is how identity systems fail.

Your verdict 05 · the market maker

A market that starts with a dime

You hated the subsidized market maker, and your requirement killed it cleanly: markets must be creatable like social posts, with less than a dollar in them, finding their price as the two sides show up. A subsidized quoting engine cannot do that, because someone has to fund the quote before the crowd exists. So that design dies on this page too. What replaces it is old enough to have a patina. In 1867 a Parisian named Joseph Oller needed racetrack betting without a bookmaker setting odds, and his answer was the parimutuel pool: everyone bets into a shared pot, the odds are simply the ratio of the two sides, and winners split the whole pot pro-rata. No quoter. No inventory. Nobody to bleed.

Look at what that structure does to every problem from the bleeding-maker section. The adverse selection that killed the automated maker does not exist, because no one is quoting stale prices for informed traders to pick off; a sharp bettor moving the odds is not extracting from a liquidity provider, they are just betting against the other side, which is the entire social contract of betting. The cold start does not exist, because the pot needs no seed: the first dime in IS the market, and the implied odds appear with the second bet. And the social-post property you wanted falls out for free: creating a market is posting a question plus your own opening bet at whatever size you like, and sharing the link. Your dime is the listing fee, the seed, and the dare, all at once.

Illustrative, with a 2% pool fee feeding the juror budget. Payouts are pro-rata shares of the whole pot, so each side's quoted payout is live and self-balancing: when YES gets crowded, betting NO gets juicy, which is what pulls the other side in.

The honest tradeoff, stated rather than hidden: a pool gives you no guaranteed exit before resolution, and your effective odds drift until close, which is why racetracks flash the odds until the gate opens. For the viral, short-dated, social questions you are describing, that costs nearly nothing, since nobody day-trades a bet on whether their friend shows up on time. For markets that grow up, the design already owns the answer: positions in the pool are claims on YES or NO, and this protocol's whole premise is that outcomes are plain tokens. So when a pool crosses a volume threshold, tokenize the claims and open an order book beside it, where professionals price their own risk. Pools for birth, books for adulthood, and at no point does a subsidized quoting engine stand in the middle getting harvested.

The design I would ship

Parimutuel birth, tokenized adulthood your requirement, met

Markets open as fee-skimmed parimutuel pools with no minimum, no seed, and no maker, so creation is as cheap as the bet you open with, and the loop chapter's calibration engine still gets its settled answer keys because pools resolve through the same optimistic layer and court. A bonus weight for early bettors, the dynamic-parimutuel idea David Pennock worked out at Yahoo in 2004, compensates the first movers who set odds with no information to lean on. Past a volume threshold the claims tokenize into the YES and NO tokens the rest of the design already runs on, an order book opens, and the original self-custody story continues unchanged. The subsidized scoring rule from round three is withdrawn. This is better, and it is better specifically because your requirement was a constraint I should have started from.

Your question 06 · the ungradable court

My recommendation, as a decision

You asked what I would actually do, so here it is as five commitments rather than a tour of options. Ship the subjective court as a separate product with its own name and its own track record, never borrowing the market court's statistics, because they measure a different skill. Open it narrow: only disputes with checkable rubric items, did the files match the spec, does the photo match the listing, where most of the judgment is verification with a thin subjective rim. Grade jurors on three signals that exist without an answer key: coherence with their own rubric scores, accuracy at predicting the panel's split, which is the peer-prediction trick that pulls signal out of questions with no ground truth, and survival of their verdicts through the appeal ladder. Tell both parties, in the contract they sign before filing, exactly what they are buying: the considered judgment of a panel of verified strangers applying a published rubric, which is your customer-satisfaction standard, adopted, because for this class of question the panel's judgment is the product. And graduate to open-ended he-said-she-said disputes only after the narrow court has a few thousand cases of appeal and overturn data to prove its graders work.

One routing rule holds the whole two-court system together: every incoming question is classified by whether reality will ever grade it, and the classification decides the court, the grader, and the promise made to the parties. Try it.

…

The router is one question deep: will reality post the answer? Everything else, the grader, the calibration source, and what the parties are promised, follows from that single bit.

And the reconciliation that ends our popularity argument for good: you were arguing for the right standard in the right court. Where reality stays silent, the crowd's considered judgment is the truth, satisfaction is the metric, and the panel is an honest sample of it. Where reality will speak, the court's only product is agreeing with her early, and there the lone correct dissenter is not an ideal, she is inventory. The system needs both courts, labeled honestly, and now it has them.

Where round four leaves us

The ledger, after the funeral

Two designs died: the market-set seat deposit and the subsidized market maker, both killed by your cold-start argument, which turned out to be the same argument twice. Volume first, friction never, let participation itself be the security and the liquidity. One argument of mine died with them, the strawman version of your petition, replaced by a corrected analysis that made the gate better than either of us had it. The cryptography turned out to be a shopping list with one watched item, the face check graded out as deployable with named limits, and the subjective court got a decision instead of a discussion.

Four rounds ago this was a headline with open windows. It is now a parts list with one watched component and a launch sequence whose first juror pays nothing, whose first market costs a dime, and whose first dispute is priced by arithmetic instead of vibes. I have nothing left on my list to attack. The next person to break something should be a user.
