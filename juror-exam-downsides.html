Design critique

The case against a proctored juror exam

A webcam entrance test, graded by an existing juror, is meant to raise quality. Several of its side effects quietly undo the properties the rest of the court depends on.

Threat model 9 min read • Deep dive · off Chapter 3

There is an obvious way to keep careless jurors out of the box. Test them at the door before they ever serve.

The proposal is specific: before you can serve, you must pass an exam on how to resolve disputes, taken live over webcam, with an already-verified juror acting as the proctor who grades you.

The aim is good. A court is only as good as the people in the box, and an entrance test promises to keep out the careless. My worry is that this particular instrument, synchronous, peer-graded, and on camera, imports problems that are larger than the one it solves. A few of them attack the exact things that make the rest of the design work: open onboarding, juror anonymity, and the absence of a single point of control. I will go through them in order of how much they worry me.

Downside 1

It rebuilds the bottleneck you spent personhood to remove

The court's onboarding is meant to be permissionless. You verify a World ID, opt in, and you are in the pool. A live webcam exam turns that into a synchronous, human-rate-limited step. Throughput is now capped by how many proctor-hours exist, not by how many people want to serve. That cap matters because of the pay model: the clearing fee floats with the ratio of demand to available jurors, so throttling supply pushes the surge multiplier up and lengthens resolution time. The exam makes the court slower and more expensive at exactly the moments demand spikes, which is when you most need supply to be elastic.

There is also a cold-start problem with no clean answer. The first proctors cannot have been certified by anyone, so the gate has to begin with a hand-picked seed and a chain of who-certifies-the-certifiers that never quite bottoms out. And most casual jurors will simply not schedule a video call with a stranger to earn a few dollars a case. You keep the committed minority and lose the long tail, which shrinks the very pool that random draws and error-averaging rely on.

Downside 2

The proctor becomes the capture surface you removed

Going from one token one vote to one human one vote took away capture by wealth. The exam puts a control point back: incumbents now decide who joins. This is easy to underrate, because the votes themselves are still random and anonymous. The trouble is that your expected share of any panel scales with your share of the pool, so whoever shapes who gets in shapes the expected majority without ever touching a sitting juror.

That turns the gate into a Sybil amplifier at the social layer. An adversary who becomes a cluster of proctors, or who pays a few, can certify a steady stream of aligned real humans, each one a genuine World ID holder, and tilt outcomes over time. Bribing jurors may be off the table; bribing the gate is a fresh and easier market, and I will come back to it.

The softer failure is just as corrosive. Proctors pass candidates who reason the way they do, so over many cycles the pool drifts toward a monoculture and dissenting styles get filtered at the door. A single proctor's pass or fail is also a subjective call, and inter-rater agreement on judgment-heavy assessments is low, so two equally able candidates get different verdicts depending on who they drew. Finally, a credentialed pool has every reason to stay small to keep fees high. The occupational licensing literature is consistent on this: licenses restrict supply and raise prices. A juror guild would behave the same way.

Downside 3

A private call creates a market in passes and in seats

Your premise is that juror bribery fails because of volume, randomness, and anonymity. The exam opens a different trade: bribing the gatekeeper rather than the juror. A one-to-one webcam call has no third party watching, so a proctor can sell passes and the collusion is close to undetectable. You have moved the bribe from a target you made unbribable to one you just created.

There is a second market. Once a certified juror seat earns money, the seat becomes an asset. A capable person can pass the exam and then rent or sell access to the identity, handing the key to someone, or something, that actually serves. World ID aims for one human one account, but attaching earning power to a credential raises the payoff to delegating or renting that account, which erodes both the personhood guarantee and the quality the exam was supposed to protect. The test can manufacture the very money-in-the-system problem the design set out to avoid.

Downside 4 · the one I weigh most

The webcam quietly reopens bribery and coercion

It breaks the anonymity the security argument rests on load-bearing

The claim that bribery is impossible leans on jurors being anonymous and unpredictable. A webcam exam shows a candidate's face to at least one other person, the proctor, and almost certainly to a recording. The moment a juror's face is known to others, they can be found and approached later, which is to say bribed, coerced, or threatened. That is the exact vector you assumed away, walked back in through the onboarding door.

Recordings make it worse. A stored library of juror faces is a deanonymization database and a high-value breach target, and a leak cannot be undone, because you cannot reissue someone's face. In places where markets touch political or criminal questions, being identifiable as a juror is a safety risk rather than a privacy footnote. You can try to wall the proctor's identity off from the juror's with separate keys and no on-chain link, but the camera itself is the leak. A face shown to a stranger is a biometric handed to a stranger, and no key hygiene fixes that.

Downside 5

Standardizing jurors can lower the panel's accuracy

The accuracy case for panels rests on jurors making independent errors. That is what lets a majority of seven beat a single expert. A shared exam, written and graded by an overlapping set of proctors, correlates the jurors: they carry the same doctrine, the same templates, and the same blind spots. Correlated errors collapse the number of effectively independent votes, so a panel of seven trained alike behaves like far fewer, and the accuracy you paid for with panel size quietly shrinks.

The sharper cost shows up on ambiguous or novel cases. A monoculture tends to be wrong together precisely when you needed a spread of priors to catch the mistake. For a body whose job is finding the truth, diversity of perspective is not noise to be sanded off in training. It is part of how the group gets to the right answer. An exam that selects for one approved way of reasoning trades some of that away.

Downside 6

A video exam starves and skews the pool

A synchronous webcam test silently filters on a working camera, stable bandwidth, a quiet private room, a usable time slot across time zones, the exam's language, literacy, and disability access. That filter falls hardest on low-income and rural users, who in the World ID population skew heavily toward the global south. That is the same group that supplies cheap, highly available juror labor and a good part of the pool's geographic and viewpoint range. So you lose supply where it is most elastic, push the clearing fee up, and tilt the bench toward wealthy, well-connected, English-speaking jurors.

There is a content problem layered on top. How to resolve a dispute is partly a normative question, not a factual one, so any exam encodes one culture's idea of sound reasoning and then selects for conformity to it. For a court meant to judge global disagreements, narrowing entry to people who can sit a video exam in the grader's language is both unfair and, by the argument above, less accurate.

Downside 7

It tests knowledge, but the failure you have is effort

The instrument and the problem do not match opportunity cost

Once bribery is gone, the live failure modes are inattention, noise on genuinely hard cases, and base competence. A one-time entrance exam touches only the last of these, and only at the instant of the test. It does nothing to make anyone read the next case carefully. The coherence reward and the bond already do that, continuously, at the moment a juror acts.

The signal is also weak and easy to fake. Passing a test on dispute-resolution principles predicts judging real, messy cases only loosely. Question banks leak into memorized answer keys. An off-camera coach, or simply a language model open in another window, can feed answers in real time, and a human proctor on a webcam cannot reliably tell. The person who passes need not be the person who later serves. So you pay heavily, in throughput and in every harm above, for a noisy one-time reading of the wrong variable, when a running measure of each juror's coherence over time targets diligence directly and for almost nothing.

Downside 8

Faces on camera bring real legal and security exposure

Recording webcam video makes you a handler of biometric and special-category data. Under GDPR Article 9 and laws like Illinois BIPA, that pulls in consent, retention, and liability duties with real penalties, and moving the data across borders multiplies the obligations. A store of juror faces is a breach target whose loss is permanent. The one-to-one call is its own attack surface too: a hostile proctor can phish for wallet access or lean on a nervous candidate, with little recourse afterward. And the gate needs its own appeals path, because people will contest failures, which is more cost and a small court growing inside the court. None of this exists in the permissionless version.

Where this leaves me

The goal is right, the instrument is the most fragile option

Pull the threads together and a pattern shows up. Most of these are not ordinary costs you can pay down. They remove the properties the rest of the system is built on: open onboarding, anonymity, decentralization, and the error independence that makes panels accurate. The exam chases real quality, but a synchronous, peer-graded, on-camera gate is close to the most expensive and most brittle way to chase it.

If quality is the goal, gentler tools beat it on every axis. Score each juror's coherence over time and weight or retire them by that record. Run new jurors through a short probation where their votes are shadowed before they count. If you want a knowledge check, make it asynchronous and automated, with no human proctor and no live camera, and feed the result into reputation rather than using it as a wall. Seed calibration questions with known answers into real caseloads to measure jurors without a separate test at all. Each of these keeps onboarding open and jurors anonymous while still pushing quality up.

If a gate is truly wanted, the least bad version drops the live webcam for a privacy-preserving async check, never lets one proctor decide alone, keeps proctor and juror identities unlinked, and treats the outcome as a weight rather than a barrier. But the honest reading is that the exam as described takes more than it gives.
