Zero to one

The launch plan: bootstrapping demothemis with predictionmomo

A new court has no cases, no money, and no track record, and each gap keeps the others open. This chapter lays out the way in: predictionmomo manufactures paid cases and public proof from day one, free juror seats and dime markets remove every entry cost, and real-world disputes follow once the record exists.

Go to market 7 min read • Act III · Chapter 6 of 10

A court is only trusted once it has a track record, but nobody pays for an untested court. predictionmomo breaks that loop. It hands the court real cases and real money on day one.

The trap is circular: People will trust a court once it has a long record of fair calls, but it cannot build a record until people bring it cases, and nobody brings real cases to a court with no record.

Worse, judges want to be paid, and good ones get better only by ruling on real disputes. So on day one you are short of all three things at once: cases, money, and a reason for anyone to believe the verdicts. Subsidies can paper over the money for a while, but a court propped up by a grant that runs out is not a business. You need a source of genuine disputes that pays for itself from the start.

The way in

The first paying customer is a bet

predictionmomo breaks the loop because it manufactures disputes that people will pay to have settled, without waiting for a single outside customer. Someone wants to bet on an election, a match, a price at a given hour. To pay out, the market has to decide what actually happened, and that decision is a dispute with money already riding on it.

Run predictionmomo on demothemis and three things fall into place at once. It needs answers, so the court always has cases. Traders pay fees to bet, so there is money to pay the jurors. And every market that settles leaves behind a known outcome, which is exactly the kind of graded case the court needs to prove and sharpen its jurors. The first chapter of demand is not a marketplace begging for users. It is people who already want to bet, handing the court paid work as a side effect.

Where the money comes from

Fees pay the judges, not a printing press

The pay for jurors comes from use, not a treasury. A 2% fee is skimmed from every pool at settlement, split once and printed: 70% to juror pay, 20% to the reward pool that pays active, high-quality jurors, 10% to operations. That pay pool is then spread across real work and real jurors. More demand with the same jurors raises pay pressure; more jurors spread it out. Careless rulings slash the live reserve built from retained fees. No salary and no entry price are hard-coded. The court breathes with traffic.

The climb

Three phases, each standing on the last

predictionmomo is the first rung, not the destination. The track record it builds is what lets the court step out into the wider world of disputes, one phase at a time.

  1. 1

    Markets only

    demothemis settles predictionmomo markets and nothing else. Anyone opens a market, public pool or private room, for the price of their own first bet, a juror's seat is free because the bond is fronted, and the first false assertion pays the watcher who catches it. Fees pay the jurors, settled markets grade them, and a public history of fair, fast calls starts to pile up.

  2. 2

    Open the doors to real disputes

    With a record in hand, the protocol starts taking outside escrowed deals. Small on-chain work comes first, judged against published checklists in the rubric court, where the funds are already locked and a refund or release hinges on whether the work matched the spec. An app that needs a neutral arbiter routes the deal through demothemis instead of promising to obey a verdict later. Each settled dispute adds to its own record, kept separate from the objective court's, because they measure different skills.

  3. 3

    Become the default

    Once enough apps route funds through the same protocol escrow, signed verdict receipts and measured court accuracy become proof the right parties can verify. New apps plug in rather than build their own support desk, and the ones that keep custody inside their own walls start to look like they have something to hide. That is the standard the vision described.

Why not just pay jurors

A subsidy is a countdown, a market is an engine

The obvious shortcut is to skip predictionmomo and pay jurors out of a treasury until customers show up. The trouble is that a treasury is a countdown. Spend it and the court goes dark unless paying demand arrived in time, and paying demand is the very thing that takes a track record to earn. You would be racing a clock you set yourself.

predictionmomo changes the math because it is demand that already exists. People bet whether or not a court needs bootstrapping, so the cases and the fees show up on their own, and the calibration data the court needs to prove itself is a free byproduct of markets settling. predictionmomo is not a marketing cost to be burned through. It is the part of the machine that keeps the rest fed.
