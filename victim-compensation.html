Design note

Should a court pay its victims?

The instinct is to make the wronged whole. This note argues that a pool which pays victims protects no one the court does not already protect, and hands anyone who controls the facts a pot to drain, with honest jurors the whole time. The better design punishes cheats, rewards the accurate, and splits a cheat's forfeited bond across the active, high-quality jurors. Every claim is laid out so you can test it yourself.

Mechanism design 13 min read • Two attacks, told apart

Start with the kindest line in the whole design and watch two very different attackers read it. A court that judges honestly, and on the rare day it is fooled reaches into a fund and makes the loser whole, sounds like justice finished properly. One attacker tries to corrupt the jurors and finds he cannot. The other never touches a juror and walks off with the fund.

This note is about telling those two apart, because the design keeps bundling them together, and the bundle is the whole reason the fund looks safe. The claim it argues for sounds harsh and turns out to be the more durable, and in the long run the kinder, design: punish jurors who cheat, reward jurors who are accurate, and run no pool that pays the victims of a bad verdict. Every step has a slider or a switch. The numbers are illustrative, the shapes are the argument.

The attack you imagine

Attack one: bribing the jury, the threat that is already stopped

The attack everyone pictures is capture: Pay off enough jurors, or pack the panel with your own people, until the verdict reads the way you want. If that worked, a court really would need a fund to clean up after it. It does not work, and it is worth seeing exactly why before we mention funds at all, because a verdict that cannot be bought needs no insurance against being bought.

To flip a verdict you must own a majority of a randomly drawn panel. You cannot buy the jurors who get drawn, because the sealed re-keyable ballot lets each one take your money and still vote their own mind, with no way for you to check. So you are forced to own the pool itself, and a wide pool turns that into a lottery you lose. On the largest cases several panels must agree at once, which multiplies an already tiny chance. Push the stakes up and the odds get worse for the attacker, not better, because the prize buys a bigger jury and more juries, never an easier one.

Drag the stakes to the ceiling and the chance only shrinks. Capture is a prevention problem, and prevention solves it without paying anyone a cent.

Nothing in that gauntlet is a payout to a victim. The verdict stays honest because of prevention, the draw and the sealed ballot and the wide pool, with the slash and the lifetime ban waiting for any single juror who sells out anyway. The court's integrity is built in, not insured after the fact. So if a compensation fund is justified, it is not here, propping up the verdict. Hold that thought, because the attacker who actually wants the fund is not trying to corrupt anyone at all.

The attack you don't

Attack two: the manufactured victim, with every juror honest

Here is the attacker who never appears in the last diagram, because he never goes near a juror. He controls the thing the market is about. Picture a company deciding whether to sell an asset, where he owns both the decision and the moment the world is told. The bet is "will it sell by the end of July," and he is the only person who knows the real answer and its timing.

He takes both sides of his own market with two wallets, no identity needed, since betting is open to anyone. The deadline passes with no announcement, so the market settles honestly: no sale was visible, the answer is no, his no wallet collects and his yes wallet loses, and his money round-trips home minus fees. If anyone disputes it, the jurors look at the facts in front of them and rule no, correctly, because on the evidence available they are right. Then he reveals the sale had quietly happened all along, calls that honest no verdict the wrong that hurt the yes side he is still holding, and asks the fund to make him whole. Flip the pool on and off and watch the only number that matters.

Read what just happened. Every juror was honest at every step. There was no panel to capture and no ballot to buy, so not one defense from the first diagram even applies. The attacker's entire profit came out of the pool, unlocked by a loss he manufactured by controlling the data and its timing. That is why the gauntlet is beside the point for the fund: the fund's weakness was never dishonest jurors. It is that a pot which pays on a claim of harm can be drained by anyone who can produce the appearance of harm, and a person who controls the facts can produce it at will. Turn the pool off and the same attack earns nothing but a fee bill. The cleanest defense against an attack that needs a pool is to keep no pool.

Two smaller locks back this up and are worth stating so the footnote is not hidden. Settled markets never reopen for new evidence, so the September revelation cannot rewrite a July payout. And a market's question should be pinned to a fact that is verifiable by its deadline, so a sale concealed until later correctly resolves no and a late reveal changes nothing. But the load-bearing answer is the simplest one: a fund that pays no claims cannot be farmed by a forged one.

The leftover money

What to do with a caught cheat's bond

Retire the victim fund and a smaller question is left over. A juror who is caught still forfeits a bond, and that money has to go somewhere. Burning it was the tidy answer in a system with its own token, where destroying supply quietly hands value to everyone still holding one. This system settles in DAI, so burning is just setting dollars alight for nobody. The other obvious destinations each switch on a bad incentive, except one. Click through them.

The even split is doing four quiet jobs at once. It cannot be claimed by a stranger, because only a verified, bonded, active juror ever receives it, so it is no honeypot. It pays nothing for any particular verdict, because your share is the same whether you sat the panel, voted yes, or voted no, so it bends no vote. It rewards simply being in the pool, which widens the pool, and pool width is the security parameter the whole court rests on. And because the reserve comes from the careless and lands on everyone, it runs a steady transfer from the rarely-honest to the usually-honest.

The one cost to weigh is that jurors, as a class, now gain a little when slashes happen. But every juror is also a potential slashee, and the triggers are mechanical rules, not a vote, so no one can profitably steer a verdict toward a slash. What is left is punishment recycled into the thing that makes the court strong: a wider, better-paid pool of honest humans. If you want even that mild class incentive gone, send forfeits to the watchers who catch cheats instead, which pays for proven detection and raises the odds the next cheat is caught. Either way, the reserve never becomes a pot a stranger can claim.

But the wronged

Will people trust a court that will not pay them?

This is the fair objection, and it deserves a real answer rather than a brave one. People use protocol escrow because it protects the money in the deal. Tell them there is no extra compensation pot beyond releasing or refunding that escrow and some of them walk. There are two answers, and the second is the one that should settle it.

First, protection can still be bought. It just should not be pooled inside the protocol and paid out on autopilot. An insurer can underwrite the risk as a separate, opt-in product, price it to the real danger, and refuse a claim that smells staged, because a private underwriter has every reason to investigate that a blind protocol fund has none. Insurance as a business someone chooses and pays for is fine. Insurance as a reflex wired into the core, paying any claim that clears a rule, is the honeypot from the second diagram. The difference is who carries the cost of being wrong: an underwriter who can say no, or a shared pool that cannot.

Second, and this is the argument that should settle it, a protocol with no compensation pot to chase is attacked less, and a protocol attacked less produces fewer victims in the first place. Compensation treats the harm after it is done. Strength removes the cause. Run the two courts side by side over a few years and the kindest-looking one does worse by the only measure that counts.

Both courts suffer the same honest mistakes. Only the paying court grows a second source of victims, the people farmed to drain its pool, and that source compounds as word spreads. It compensates some of them and creates more.

The paying court spends five years making some victims whole and manufacturing more of them than it ever pays. The punishing court has nothing worth staging an attack for, so the only victims it ever sees are the honest mistakes, and there are fewer of those because there are fewer attacks. The kindest thing a court can do for victims is to have fewer of them.

The verdict

Punish the guilty, reward the honest, and keep no vault

A court has two jobs that survive contact with an attacker. Make a bought verdict impossible, and make a careless one expensive. The first diagram showed the first job done by prevention; the slash and the lifetime ban do the second. Both are clean. One pays jurors from fees for honest work, the other takes money from a cheat and spreads it across the honest pool. Neither is a pot a stranger can open with a story.

The fund was quietly trying to do a third job, insure against bad verdicts, and the second diagram showed that job is a trap, because the only person with a real motive to collect is the one who manufactured the loss. So the answer to the question in the title is no. Not because victims do not matter, but because the surest way to protect them is a court too solid to be captured and too disciplined to keep a vault that only a fraudster has a reason to visit.

One honest line to close on. The design takes exactly this route. No pool pays a victim, the slash and the lifetime ban stay, and a caught cheat's forfeited reserve joins the reward pool that pays the active, high-quality jurors. The reasoning above is the why, settled before any pool could grow large enough to be worth draining.
