010:00 – 0:25Sandbox · the hook

Buy this verdict

Every dispute oracle shares one flaw: in a naive stake-vote, whoever owns the most tokens owns the verdict. Same question, same attacker budget — $200,000 buys 63% of the stake, and it's bought — and a bought majority stays bought, so the next verdict is free. DemoThemis answers differently: one verified human, one vote, a random panel drawn only after the question — so the same budget can't target the panel; it would have to corrupt a near-majority of the whole pool for only a ~3% chance, and the verdict holds. And nothing carries over — every new case means fresh bribes from scratch, and any panel you somehow do capture just gets appealed into a bigger one.

Illustrative: a naive stake-vote, not UMA/Kleros (which add defenses), and DemoThemis's multi-panel security is its design; the live MVP is a single 3-seat demo panel, and security scales with pool width.

020:25 – 0:45Sandbox · the math

Why it can't be bought

Why can't you just buy it? Because there's nothing to buy. Flipping a token court needs half the stake — a fixed price, paid once, reusable forever. DemoThemis draws the panel at random after the question, so you can't know who to bribe. To land a bought majority on one case you'd have to pre-corrupt a near-majority of the entire human pool and hope the draw seats enough of them — and pay it all again, from scratch, on the next case. And above a value line, panels stack — 7, then 15, then 31 — and because each independent panel must all agree, the capture odds multiply, so the cost climbs with the pot. The token court flips at 100%. The human court barely moves — and, by design, gets costlier as the pool, the case volume, and the pot grow. Buying a verdict means buying a crowd you can't see. (That "rises with the pot" curve is the parallel-panel design; the live MVP is a single 3-seat panel.)

The unlock: a real human jury, on-chain — and why now

A one-human-one-vote court couldn't exist on-chain until World ID 4.0 moved verification on-chain. UMA and Kleros decide by capital because that primitive didn't exist when they were built — so this is why it's a World project, and why now. Watch it inside the transaction: the registry calls the gate, the gate calls World's on-chain verifier, the personhood proof is checked live, and the bond moves through a MiniKit/Permit2 sponsored batch. A forged proof reverts; a second wallet for the same human reverts — the nullifier is tied to the person, not the wallet, so 10,000 wallets buy zero seats.

And the proof only works from your wallet — a stolen World ID proof reverts before the check. (World ID is the strongest on-chain personhood primitive; it resists Sybil farms, not the bribery of distinct humans.) This worldscan view is an illustrative reconstruction of the capstone registration: no live registration exists yet — jurorCount is still zero, and the proven on-chain traces to date use the Staging verifier.

The verdict moves the money — in one transaction

When the panel reveals, the majority decides — and the same transaction releases the money: resolve → escrow.settle, one atomic step, no claim button, no marshal, no one in between. (UMA and Kleros emit a ruling something else must act on; a platform is a human clicking refund.) Three verified humans, one vote each, settled the 500-MUSD deal. The fee splits 70/20/10, and a convicting majority never earns the loser's bond — no profit in railroading a dissenter.

Atomic over the funds the escrow already holds; a real-world delivery still needs an honest party or oracle.

10—How it compares · the landscape

DemoThemis vs UMA, Kleros & the platforms

How does it stack up? UMA and Kleros are credible, transparent, and have real defenses — but both decide by capital: UMA is one-token-one-vote, Kleros weights juror draws by stake, so influence tracks wealth. A contested 2025 UMA resolution — the $160M Polymarket "Zelensky suit" market — drew public accusations of large-holder influence; disputed, not proven, but the concern is real. Centralized arbiters like Airbnb or eBay are fast and familiar, but the company that profits from the deal also judges it. Being honest: all three decentralized courts run on the same Schelling-coherence incentive, and UMA and Kleros are mature and well-defended — Kleros even wins on appeal. DemoThemis's categorical difference is who decides: verified-distinct humans, one human one vote, a fresh panel every case — not stake.

One caveat on this table: the personhood and unknowable-pool advantages are mainnet-only — today's live MVP runs a mock cohort behind a single 3-seat panel, and security scales with pool width, so parallel panels, the appeal ladder, reputation, and receipt-free ballots are still roadmap. And the "no override" claim is source-verified: the only deployer powers are one-time wiring plus the phase clock for future cases — no pause, no verdict override, no fund-seizure — though the contracts are unaudited and non-upgradeable.

11—Cost to corrupt · the math

Capital has a ceiling. Personhood doesn't.

The security models differ at the root. UMA's safety is one global inequality — market cap above twice the profit from corruption — true on average, but it thins on the single biggest question. Kleros makes capture costly with escalating appeals, yet it's still a contest of stake. DemoThemis prices corruption per question: the panel is drawn at random after the question, so there's no panel to buy — you'd have to bribe a near-majority of every human in the pool and merely hope the draw seats enough of them, for a probability still under one. In a 200,000-strong pool that's ~$650k for a coin flip on a single case — versus ~$200k to own a token court forever — and DemoThemis re-pays it every case while the token court reuses its buy. On the chart the two lines cross at ≈62,000 jurors: past that, one DemoThemis case already costs more than owning the token court forever, and the gap widens with every case. By design several independent panels must all agree, so those odds multiply pᴺ. On mainnet today that's a single 3-seat demo panel — the pool-width and multiplied security are the funded design — but capture already never carries to the next case.

The honest floor here is $6.50 a juror — a $1.50 fee plus a $5 bond, with reputation valued at zero in the MVP — and the permanent World-ID ban pushes it higher. And the MVP's 3-of-3 demo is the one degenerate spot where the pool equals the panel, so about $13 flips it — a liveness and cost demo, not the real security cost.

12—Game theory · vs Airbnb/eBay/Uber

The judge was never paid to be right

The biggest flaw isn't that these platforms get hacked — it's that the judge was never paid to be right. When Airbnb, eBay, or Uber settle a dispute, the company that profits from it is also the arbiter, rewarded for retention and lower cost, not the truth — so the ruling can tilt toward either side, whichever protects its revenue. DemoThemis hands the decision to bonded, disinterested humans with no stake in the marketplace — and routes every slash to a neutral pool, never the winning jurors.

Building a marketplace? Don't run disputes yourself — you'd start conflicted, both sides assuming you protect your revenue, and you'd pay to staff it. Start on a neutral court instead: "an impartial court decides, not us" is a trust advantage a new brand can't fake, and the integration is two functions — open a dispute, receive the verdict. You grow the marketplace; the court keeps it fair.

14—Landing · the close

A court you can't simply buy

DemoThemis: one verified human, one vote — decided by people with no stake in either party. Not capital, like UMA and Kleros. Not the platform's own desk, like Airbnb or eBay. The design is a credibly-neutral court that's far costlier to buy, pack, or capture — the panel is drawn at random after the question, so you'd have to corrupt a near-majority of the entire human pool, every single case, for only a chance, and it gets costlier the more the verdict is worth. The capstone-ready MVP proves the mechanism with a 3-seat demo panel; that resistance scales with pool width. Watch the attack in the sandbox, scan the QR, and file a dispute yourself — verify in World App over a sponsored-gas path, with valueless MockUSD.
