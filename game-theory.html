Break the court

Try to break the court.

You are the attacker for five minutes. Pick a scenario, drag the knobs, and see whether a coordinated group, provable bribe, rented account, lazy vote, delay attack, or crowd-following can make money.

The loop is simple: cases create fees, fees pay jurors, paid jurors widen the available jury pool, and a wider pool makes bad strategies harder. This is a sandbox, not a launch forecast or measured failure rate. When something breaks, the cards tell you why; when it holds, Scenario checks and Assumptions show the settings doing the work.

Assumptions behind the verdict

The main board stays quick. This tab shows the hidden sliders, confidence tags, dependency map, formulas, and assumptions that most change the result.

Advanced assumptionsdefaults

These sliders show the hidden modeling factors behind the safety score. They are tunable launch assumptions, not measured failure rates. Governance is intentionally excluded from this MVP simulation; protocol-critical rules are treated as fixed launch rails here.

What moves what. Each output below lists the knobs and assumptions that can change it.

Knob guide

What each knob changes

Money disputed each month

More disputed money creates more cases and more fees to pay jurors.

Active jurors

More jurors widen the available jury pool. Heavy money-at-risk requirements can push some out.

Typical case size

Bigger cases use larger juries, stricter eligibility, and sometimes multiple juries.

Where fees go

Fees can fund immediate juror pay, long-term rewards, and operations.

Juror pay

Higher pay makes juror work more attractive, helping recruit and retain honest jurors. It is not a guaranteed wage.

Money at risk

Pending juror income can be lost for careless voting. Too little invites laziness; too much shrinks supply.

Long-term rewards

Rewards pay quality over time instead of paying any one verdict to punish dissent.

Random jury draw

Turns jury selection from retryable grinding into one public random draw bound to the case.

Private vote

Weakens bribes. If a briber cannot prove how someone voted, the bribe contract is hard to enforce.

Live face check

Turns a rented account into a live-person check at the moment the vote unlocks.

Aptitude tests

Resolved cases for aptitude tests give the court an outside signal against jurors simply following the crowd.

Appeal cost floor

Each appeal must pay for the next jury, capture risk, and the delay it creates.

Appeal sincerity gate

Loser-side signatures make appeals costly enough to deter spam without letting winners block appeals.

Quick math

The math, briefly

Money flow

fee revenue = disputed money × case fee
juror pay pool = fee revenue × juror-pay share
reward pool = fee revenue × reward share
retained jurors respond to pay, rewards, and money at risk

Money at risk

risk per vote = pay per vote / (lazy-vote mistakes × penalty size)
live money at risk = risk per vote × pending votes per juror
if the burden gets too heavy, the available juror pool thins

Jury-control odds

usable attackers = coordinated attackers who qualify + rented accounts that pass checks
control chance = finite draw chance of an attacker majority
large cases automatically require multiple juries

Safety score

score rises with juror accuracy, aptitude tests, and reward-funded retention
score falls with active risks, and any attack that works caps the score

Risk ranking

What is closest to breaking

The highest bar is the first place to inspect. If attackers can control a jury, widen the available juror pool or use harder jury rules. If jurors follow the crowd, add aptitude tests. If lazy voting gets paid, increase the money at risk.

The takeaway

The goal: bad attacks should lose money

More money at risk is not automatically safer. A higher fee is not automatically better. A bigger jury is not free. A stricter gate can make jurors copy the crowd. Every knob has a job and a cost.

The model looks healthiest when cases pay enough honest jurors, aptitude tests keep jurors honest, and every attack costs more than it can win.
