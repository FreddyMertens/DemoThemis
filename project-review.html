DemoThemis & PredictionMoMo

The big idea, how it could work, what is already built, and what still needs proof.

Project status: deployed MVP, not production-ready.

A source-verified World Chain slice includes World ID registration wiring, commit/reveal voting, escrow settlement, and fee routing with valueless test tokens. The live three-human capstone, receipt-free ballots, production randomness, hardened appeals and reputation, and an independent audit are still unfinished.

Thirty-second answer

What is this project?

People lock money around a question. If they disagree about the answer, randomly selected, verified people study the evidence, vote, and decide who receives the money.

Product one

PredictionMoMo

A proposed prediction market where people put money on answers such as YES or NO. Think of it as a shared betting jar whose payout depends on what really happens.

Product two

DemoThemis

A human-juror court design with a deployed MVP slice. When people disagree, randomly selected, verified humans review evidence and decide the result.

Why combine them? The market supplies cases and fees; the court supplies neutral resolution. Escrow keeps the money locked until the decision is final.
Idea and explanationStrong, unusual, clearly taught, and worth testing.
Deployed MVPA real on-chain slice exists; its small panel and current ballot are demonstrations, not the full security design.
Production readinessNot ready for real money: core mechanisms, live usage, and outside review remain unfinished.

The problem

Why build a new referee?

Online deals and prediction markets need a trusted answer when people disagree. A company-controlled referee may favor the company. A token vote may give the richest wallets the most power.

DemoThemis tries a different base rule: one verified human can hold one juror seat. The MVP wires World ID into registration; the full design still needs to prove that identity, privacy, recovery, and juror selection work safely together.

Important limit “One human, one seat” can make it harder to create thousands of fake jurors. It does not prove that a person is honest, skilled, independent, awake, or impossible to pressure.

The proposed journey

How one market could work

  1. Create a question and lock fundsA creator writes a gradeable YES-or-NO question; participants choose sides while escrow holds the money.
  2. Trade the possible answersA shared pool shows changing odds, while fixed-price offers let traders name a price.
  3. Post a bonded answerSomeone proposes the result and risks money for being wrong. If nobody challenges it, the market settles.
  4. Challenge and draw jurorsA disputed answer triggers a random panel of verified humans, with a presence check proposed for each seat.
  5. Review, vote, and appealJurors study the evidence and the full design keeps individual votes private. A losing side may request a larger panel.
  6. Finalize and release escrowThe final answer pays the winning claims and routes the court’s fees.
Market details kept outside the main journey

Early pool positions may receive extra weight. A market that meets defined liquidity tests may later turn claims into wallet-held YES and NO tokens. Fixed offers, graduation, fee math, and token conversion still need exact conservation rules.

Objective case

Reality can later grade the answer, such as a match score, a closing price, or a delivery date.

Rubric case

Jurors compare evidence with written rules, such as whether work met a contract. There may be no perfect outside answer.

The strongest idea

How the two products could grow together

A new court has a “cold start” problem. It needs work before people trust it, but people may not send work to a court with no history. PredictionMoMo is meant to create the first useful loop.

Markets create cases
Cases create fees
Fees pay jurors
Finished cases build a record
A stronger court attracts markets

Markets create cases; cases create fees; fees pay jurors; finished cases build a record; and a stronger court attracts more markets.

Fees: The on-chain MVP routes valueless test-token fees 70% to jurors, 20% to a reward pool, and 10% to operations. The wider product proposes a 2% settlement fee and up to another 1% PredictionMoMo margin; it has no real fee revenue yet.

Resolved design decision

Private votes require private consequences

01
Resolved in design · implementation must prove it

Can a secret vote remain secret?

Yes—if exact reputation, reserves, payments, penalties, refunds, and case history also stay private. The public should see one-case pseudonyms, limited eligibility proofs, the aggregate tally, and aggregate accounting, never a named consequence that reveals a vote.

Still required: audited private commitments, batched accounting, aggregate-only tallying, and unlinkable payouts.

Remaining implementation work and evidence

The rule is “public proofs and aggregate accounting; private votes, histories, balances, reputation, penalties, refunds, and payments.” Each case uses a new pseudonym and receiving key. Zero-knowledge proofs reveal only eligibility, a reputation band, sufficient reserve, and correct private updates. Correlation checks must run over encrypted histories and reveal only a proved rule breach. Threshold encryption alone does not force aggregate-only decryption, so the commitments, batched accounting, metadata boundary, and unlinkable payout flow still require specification and independent audit.

Repository evidence: demothemis.html:463–513, 646–725.

Main critique

The most important unanswered questions

These do not mean the idea cannot work. They mean the project must answer them before it can safely hold real money.

02
Critical · finality risk

Can every case reach a final answer?

Large cases may use several juries and require every jury to agree. One honest disagreement, missing panel, or malicious holdout could stop the money from paying.

Why it mattersAn attacker may not need to control the verdict. Delaying it forever could be cheaper.
What to doDefine quorum, deadlines, replacement rules, how panels combine, and a final fallback that always ends.
Technical detail and evidence

The simulator raises one-panel capture probability to the number of panels. That models how hard it is to capture every panel, but not how easy it may be to cause one disagreement. It also leaves out shared evidence errors, juror outages, abstentions, and correlated mistakes.

Repository evidence: demothemis.html:519–551; game-theory.html:1020–1021.

03
Critical · money and token accounting

Does all the token math balance?

The early market is a shared pot with changing odds and an early-bettor bonus. The later market uses fungible YES and NO tokens. Parlays add another token model. The exact conversion equations are missing.

Why it mattersWithout conservation rules, claims may not remain fully backed or may pay differently from what users expect.
What to doPublish equations and tests for token supply, early weights, redemptions, fees, parlays, rounding, and collateral conservation.
Technical detail and evidence

An older design mints one YES and one NO for one collateral unit, while the current design starts with a weighted parimutuel pool and tokenizes later. A conversion may be possible, but it must prove that no value is created, lost, or promised twice. Composability and lending claims should wait for that proof.

Repository evidence: prediction-market.html:234–355, 386–411, 493–549; prediction-market.md:11–15.

04
Critical · evidence quality

Does the attack model test the idea, or repeat its assumptions?

The simulator is very useful for learning. It is not proof. Some protections succeed because the code assumes they succeed. The named presets are also required to pass the checks.

Why it mattersA score such as 93 out of 100 can look measured even when it is built from uncertain inputs.
What to doAdd unsafe examples, independent assumptions, real measurements, correlated failures, wider attacker ranges, and an outside red team.
Technical detail and evidence

With private ballots enabled, ordinary profitable bribery is prevented by model logic and a residual risk is assigned. The validator rejects any named preset that clears an attack or even reaches a watch state. The model caps attackers and stake sizes, and often treats juror mistakes as independent. Sixteen of 35 assumptions are labeled low-confidence or future rails; seven more are index weights or guardrails.

Repository evidence: game-theory.html:974–1062; assumptions.js; tools/validate-gamelab.js:335–358.

05
High · learning-data risk

How does the system learn the truth?

An answer that nobody challenged is not always true. It may only be small, obscure, or badly timed. Turning unnoticed mistakes into juror test questions could teach the court the wrong lesson.

Next: Separate court verdicts from outside facts, govern evidence quality and synthetic tests, and audit watchers.

Risk, next step, and evidence
Why it mattersBad training data can make a confident system consistently wrong.
What to doSeparate court verdicts from outside facts, require evidence-quality rules, audit watchers, and define who may create synthetic tests.

Private markets may also be used as hidden tests, but participants could collude to plant a false answer. Synthetic-test governance is explicitly left open. The market token can lower the curation bar even though governance copy says it cannot affect court test data, creating an indirect leak through the claimed firewall.

Repository evidence: demothemis.html:584–641; governance.html:107–123, 139–147, 237–248.

06
High · conflicts and insider knowledge

Can juror conflicts really be found?

A juror may hide an interest in another wallet or through a third party. An attacker may also send tiny unwanted tokens to a juror so that the juror looks conflicted and gets replaced.

Next: Use pre-draw snapshots, ignore unsolicited dust, hide summons, define exposure rules, and limit trading during a case.

Risk, next step, and evidence
Why it mattersReplacement rules can be attacked, while hidden interests may still pass the screen.
What to doUse a pre-draw snapshot, ignore unsolicited dust, hide summons, define exposure rules, and limit juror trading during a case.

Case-specific pseudonyms and the draw must remain auditable while outcome tokens continue trading during review. That creates targeting, chain-analysis, hedging, and possible insider-trading problems. Positions may also be hidden through custodians, lending pools, derivatives, or parlays.

Repository evidence: demothemis.html:412–438, 488–508; prediction-market.html:486–488.

07
High · governance and upgrades

Can the rules be repaired safely?

A truly unchangeable constitution cannot fix a serious bug. An upgradeable constitution needs someone or some process with upgrade power. That power can become another capture risk.

Next: Specify guarded pauses, upgrades, delays, migrations, key custody, user exits, and emergency limits.

Risk, next step, and evidence
Why it mattersFinancial software needs both strong limits and a safe way to respond to failures.
What to doDefine ownership, guardians, pauses, upgrades, delays, migrations, key custody, user exits, and emergency limits.

The court token uses square-root token weight, service multipliers, a per-person cap, and a 4% active-weight quorum. The denominator, low-turnout behavior, and manipulation of paid service history need specification. Paying fees to manufacture service is not automatically useless if the governance power gained is worth more than the burn.

Repository evidence: governance.html:153–181, 202–223, 227–248.

08
High · incomplete state machines

What happens when real life is messy?

The run-through now maps the happy path and named failure exits, but several amber exits remain deliberately unresolved. Cancelled events, missing jurors, panel disagreement, collateral failure, migration, and recovery still need executable rules.

Next: Turn every unresolved exit into a specified transition and invariant before extending the contracts.

Missing paths, next step, and evidence
Why it mattersMost serious financial bugs live in unusual transitions, not the normal path.
What to doSpecify every unresolved transition and its invariants before extending the contracts.
  • No answer, or competing answers.
  • A cancelled, delayed, partial, or ungradeable event.
  • Too few jurors, withdrawal, or panel disagreement.
  • Frozen, depegged, or bridged collateral.
  • An upgrade, pause, migration, refund, or final appeal.

Appeal timing is now aligned to a governance-configurable 2–8-week window recorded per case. The launch value, timeout transitions, bond sizing, fee rounding, dust, replay, double finalization, and emergency recovery still need tests and specification.

Repository evidence: the-design.html:1289–1347, 2024–2367; demothemis.html:698–725; governance.html:248.

09
High · external dependency

Does World ID provide every required feature?

The MVP proves on-chain verifier wiring and duplicate-nullifier rejection. It does not yet prove that real-human onboarding, draw-time presence, privacy between cases, recovery, reputation, and outages work together.

Next: Complete the human capstone, then test presence, recovery, privacy, and outage paths.

Risk, next step, and evidence
Why it mattersAn outage, false rejection, stolen phone, replay, or policy change could stop the court.
What to doComplete the three-human capstone, then test presence, recovery, privacy, and outage paths before relying on the full identity flow.

World documentation supports privacy-preserving proof of human and on-chain verification. It describes high-assurance Orb-backed uniqueness separately from Selfie Check, which is labeled beta. The source-verified MVP wires the Production verifier, while current enforcement traces use Simulator/Staging identities and no real person has completed the Production-verifier capstone. The fuller mix of persistent eligibility, draw-bound face continuity, unlinkable case proofs, private reputation commitments, and contract enforcement remains unproven.

Repository evidence: mvp.html:145–172; DemoThemisMVP/README.md:26–60; demothemis.html:441–460. External sources: World ID Overview and On-chain Verification, listed in the appendix.

10
High · law and operations

Can it legally and safely handle real money?

The public copy now says “eligible, clearly worded markets,” but eligibility still has no launch-region, topic, age, identity, sanctions, or licensing rules. Those rules differ by country.

Next: Choose one launch region, restrict market types, and obtain specialist legal, compliance, and responsible-gambling advice.

Risk, next step, and evidence
Why it mattersA technically working market can still be illegal, inaccessible, or unsafe for customers.
What to doChoose one launch region, restrict market types, and obtain specialist legal, compliance, and responsible-gambling advice.

In the United States, the CFTC says public prediction-market contracts may be swaps or futures and a public market may need designated-contract-market registration. Great Britain requires an appropriate licence to provide remote gambling to British consumers, including when the operator is based abroad. A real service also needs market surveillance, customer-fund controls, AML/sanctions processes, age rules, complaints, incident response, and operational monitoring.

External sources: CFTC 2026 Prediction Markets notice and UK Gambling Commission remote-sector guidance, listed in the appendix. This review is not legal advice.

Product, protocol, and usability

A shorter, safer development path

Keep claims and status current

Maintain the new deployed, simulated, proposed, and unfinished labels as implementation changes; keep safety claims conditional and reading times honest.

Complete one official specification

Define every role, state, deadline, failure path, equation, trust assumption, privacy promise, and upgrade rule; archive conflicting drafts.

Test the four hardest mechanisms

Stress private tally and consequence accounting, World ID presence and privacy, pool-to-token conservation, and replacement plus guaranteed finality. Include unsafe and correlated scenarios.

Harden one narrow path

Finish the three-human capstone, then prove one objective binary case with one valueless asset, production randomness, a wider panel, one appeal, receipt-free voting, failures, and final settlement.

Obtain outside review

Cryptography, contracts, economics, identity, privacy, accessibility, operations, and law all need specialist review before real funds.

Defer extra complexity. Parlays, lending collateral, court tokens, private markets, rubric courts, permanent collusion audits, and broad marketplace arbitration should wait until the narrow path works.

Short glossary
Prediction market
A place where people put money behind different answers.
Protocol
A shared rulebook carried out by software.
Escrow
Money locked until the rules say who receives it.
Collateral
Money or assets backing a promise or claim.
Token
A digital claim ticket. It is not automatically company ownership.
Assertion
Someone’s proposed final answer.
Challenge
A formal objection to the proposed answer.
Bond
Money risked to discourage dishonest or careless claims.
Juror panel
A small group selected to decide a dispute.
Verified human
A person who has proved uniqueness, not honesty or skill.
Private ballot
A vote that should not reveal how one person voted.
Appeal
A request for another, usually larger, panel to check a ruling.
Finality
The point when the decision pays and cannot reopen.
Governance
The process used to change system rules.
Aptitude test
A quiz estimating whether a juror understands a topic.
Sybil attack
One person pretending to be many people.
Smart contract
Blockchain code that moves assets according to rules.
Threshold encryption
A lock that needs several key holders to cooperate before it opens.

Final assessment

Bottom line

The market-to-court bootstrap is a strong idea, and a source-verified MVP now demonstrates World ID registration wiring, commit/reveal voting, and settlement with valueless test tokens. The full market, receipt-free privacy, production randomness, reliable finality, exact token accounting, hardened appeals and reputation, live-human evidence, and independent review remain unfinished.

In one line: promising design, real MVP slice, incomplete production protocol, and not ready for real money.
