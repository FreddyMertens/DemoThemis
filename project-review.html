DemoThemis & PredictionMoMo

The big idea, the mechanism, its strongest advantages, and the decisions that make it work.

Two products create one compounding advantage.

PredictionMoMo supplies paid disputes and clean answer keys. DemoThemis turns them into a wider, better measured, and increasingly difficult-to-buy juror network.

Thirty-second answer

What is this project?

People lock money around a question. If they disagree about the answer, randomly selected, verified people study the evidence, vote, and decide who receives the money.

Product one

PredictionMoMo

An open prediction market where people put money on answers such as YES or NO. Think of it as a shared betting jar whose payout depends on what really happens.

Product two

DemoThemis

A bullet proof human-juror court. When people disagree, randomly selected, verified humans review evidence privately and decide the result.

Why combine them? The market supplies cases and fees; the court supplies neutral resolution. Escrow keeps the money locked until the decision is final.
Open market creationAny precise question can begin with the first stake, even below one dollar.
Unbuyable arbitrationVerified people, private ballots, random panels, and costed appeals keep capital from purchasing the verdict.
Compounding demandMarket fees fund the court while curated outcomes continuously improve juror selection.

The problem

Why build a new referee?

Online deals and prediction markets need a trusted answer when people disagree. A company-controlled referee may favor the company. A token vote may give the richest wallets the most power.

DemoThemis uses a different base rule: one verified human can hold one juror seat. World ID closes duplicate identities; private ballots, live presence, aptitude scoring, and expanding panels remove the remaining shortcuts.

The defence stack One human, one seat blocks wallet multiplication. The reserve rewards care, the sealed draw blocks panel shopping, and the receipt-free ballot makes bought votes unenforceable.

The product journey

How one market could work

  1. Create a question and lock fundsA creator writes a gradeable YES-or-NO question; participants choose sides while escrow holds the money.
  2. Trade the possible answersA shared pool shows changing odds, while fixed-price offers let traders name a price.
  3. Post a bonded answerSomeone proposes the result and risks money for being wrong. If nobody challenges it, the market settles.
  4. Challenge and draw jurorsA disputed answer triggers a random panel of verified humans, with a live presence check for each seat.
  5. Review, vote, and appealJurors study the evidence while individual votes stay private. A losing side may fund a larger panel.
  6. Finalize and release escrowThe final answer pays the winning claims and routes the court’s fees.
Market details kept outside the main journey

Early pool positions receive extra weight. Once a market clears its liquidity tests, claims graduate into wallet-held YES and NO tokens backed by exact conservation, mint, burn, and settlement rules.

Objective case

Reality can later grade the answer, such as a match score, a closing price, or a delivery date.

Rubric case

Jurors compare evidence with written rules, such as whether work met a contract. There may be no perfect outside answer.

The strongest idea

How the two products could grow together

PredictionMoMo gives the court its first paid work, evidence, and public history, creating a useful loop from the first market.

Markets create cases
Cases create fees
Fees pay jurors
Finished cases build a record
A stronger court attracts markets

Markets create cases; cases create fees; fees pay jurors; finished cases build a record; and a stronger court attracts more markets.

Fees: A 2% settlement fee routes 70% to jurors, 20% to the reward pool, and 10% to operations. PredictionMoMo may add a margin of up to one percentage point after the court invoice routes first.

Resolved design decision

Private votes require private consequences

01
Resolved architecture

Can a secret vote remain secret?

Yes—if exact reputation, reserves, payments, penalties, refunds, and case history also stay private. The public should see one-case pseudonyms, limited eligibility proofs, the aggregate tally, and aggregate accounting, never a named consequence that reveals a vote.

The privacy stack: private commitments, batched accounting, aggregate-only tallying, and unlinkable payouts.

How it works and evidence

The rule is “public proofs and aggregate accounting; private votes, histories, balances, reputation, penalties, refunds, and payments.” Each case uses a new pseudonym and receiving key. Zero-knowledge proofs reveal only eligibility, a reputation band, sufficient reserve, and correct private updates. Correlation checks run over encrypted histories and reveal only a proved rule breach. Private commitments, batched accounting, aggregate-only tallying, and unlinkable payouts preserve the same boundary from ballot through reward.

Repository evidence: demothemis.html:463–513, 646–725.

The design answers

How the system closes the hardest gaps

The architecture answers finality, collateral, attack resistance, juror learning, conflicts, upgrades, complete product flow, and proof of human in separate layers.

02
Finality

Every case reaches one final answer

Published deadlines, deterministic standbys, a finite 7-to-15-to-31 appeal ladder, and one irreversible release rule give every dispute a bounded path to payment.

Why it winsDelay cannot become a cheaper substitute for buying the verdict.
The resultSettlement closes on schedule while later accountability remains open.
Technical detail and evidence

Large cases use non-overlapping parallel panels, while the finite appeal ladder and one release rule keep settlement bounded. Capturing every independent panel on the same sealed draw multiplies the attacker’s difficulty.

Repository evidence: demothemis.html:519–551; break-the-court.html:1020–1021.

03
Full collateralization

Every claim stays backed

Conservation, mint, burn, redemption, fee, rounding, and atomic parlay rules keep pooled claims and wallet tokens tied to the same locked collateral.

Why it winsNo bookmaker balance sheet sits between the claim and its backing.
The resultTokens remain composable without promising the same collateral twice.
Technical detail and evidence

The shared pool begins with fully escrowed stakes. Graduation conserves that backing as YES and NO claims move into user wallets; redemption, fees, rounding, and atomic routing all resolve against the same collateral base.

Repository evidence: predictionmomo.html:234–355, 386–411, 493–549; prediction-market.md:11–15.

04
Attack transparency

Every defence can be stress-tested

Break the Court exposes the inputs behind coalition capture, bribery, rentals, careless voting, appeal delay, and crowd-following, then recalculates every margin immediately.

Why it winsNo security claim is hidden behind a single score.
The resultReaders can attack the mechanism themselves and see which independent rail defeats each shortcut.
Technical detail and evidence

Private ballots remove provable vote delivery, live checks price account rental, random panels suppress coalition majorities, pending pay defeats careless shortcuts, and aptitude tests preserve decision quality. Every control remains editable in the stress model.

Repository evidence: break-the-court.html:974–1062; assumptions.js; tools/validate-gamelab.js:335–358.

05
Reality-anchored learning

The court learns from checkable outcomes

Watching, independent verification, and curation admit only precise, checkable outcomes into the aptitude library.

The advantage: Jurors are measured against reality instead of the court merely agreeing with itself.

Quality gates and evidence

Paid watching catches false assertions, independent verification checks the outside fact, and curation admits only clean outcomes. Private cases add consent and participant exclusion before becoming hidden tests.

Repository evidence: demothemis.html:584–641; bootstrap-loop.html:83–141.

06
Conflict resistance

Case interests are screened before voting

Pre-draw snapshots, dust-resistant exposure rules, hidden summons, deterministic replacements, and case-bound trading controls protect the panel from financial conflicts.

The advantage: Parties cannot shop the panel or disqualify honest jurors after the draw.

Conflict controls and evidence

A pre-draw exposure snapshot catches positions before the panel exists. Unsolicited dust is ignored, summons stay private, and deterministic standbys preserve the sealed draw without giving either party a replacement lever.

Repository evidence: demothemis.html:412–438, 488–508; predictionmomo.html:486–488.

07
Constitutional upgrades

Rules can improve without an emergency ruler

A narrow, public migration path combines transparent notice, delays, and user choice while the constitutional firewall remains outside either token vote.

The advantage: Faulty rules can be replaced without granting a key that can rewrite an active verdict.

Upgrade controls and evidence

Square-root person-weighted voting, a per-person cap, token locks, quorum, timelocks, hard parameter ranges, and a public migration path let the system improve without exposing active verdicts to governance.

Repository evidence: governance.html:153–181, 202–248.

08
Complete product journey

Every successful route is easy to follow

The run-through maps public pools, private escrow, token graduation, quiet settlement, disputed settlement, appeals, payout, juror learning, and the next market in one connected journey.

The advantage: Readers can trace both the fast route and the full court route from first stake to final payment.

Complete route and evidence

Four phases connect market creation, answer checking, unbuyable arbitration, and settlement. The fast path bypasses the court; the disputed path adds verified jurors and costed appeals; both converge on one final release rule.

Repository evidence: run-through.html; demothemis.html:698–725.

09
Verified-human foundation

One person receives one seat

World ID supplies unique-human verification while draw-bound face checks prove live presence without exposing names, faces, or a reusable identity handle.

The advantage: Wallet multiplication disappears, account rentals require a present human, and every case receives a fresh pseudonym.

Identity stack and evidence

Orb-backed World ID establishes uniqueness. Each selected juror then proves live presence through a short-lived, draw-bound face check while case pseudonyms and private commitments keep identity, history, and payment unlinkable.

Repository evidence: demothemis-mvp.html:145–172; demothemis.html:441–460. External sources: World ID Overview and On-chain Verification, listed in the appendix.

Product, protocol, and usability

The clearest path to scale

Keep the promise sharp

Lead with open market creation, bullet proof arbitration, self-custodied claims, and the demand loop that connects both products.

Publish one official specification

Put every role, state, deadline, equation, privacy rule, and constitutional boundary into one inspectable source.

Prove the defence stack

Stress private tallying, World ID presence, collateral conservation, deterministic replacement, appeals, and finality together.

Run the complete route

Take one objective case through escrow, random draw, receipt-free voting, a wider-panel appeal, and final settlement.

Invite specialist validation

Cryptography, contracts, economics, identity, privacy, accessibility, and operations each receive an independent review.

Short glossary
Prediction market
A place where people put money behind different answers.
Protocol
A shared rulebook carried out by software.
Escrow
Money locked until the rules say who receives it.
Collateral
Money or assets backing a promise or claim.
Token
A digital claim ticket representing a market position or governance voice.
Assertion
Someone’s proposed final answer.
Challenge
A formal objection to the proposed answer.
Bond
Money risked to discourage dishonest or careless claims.
Juror panel
A small group selected to decide a dispute.
Verified human
A unique person eligible to hold one juror seat.
Private ballot
A vote that reveals the aggregate verdict without exposing one person’s choice.
Appeal
A request for another, usually larger, panel to check a ruling.
Finality
The point when the decision pays and cannot reopen.
Governance
The process used to change system rules.
Aptitude test
A quiz estimating whether a juror understands a topic.
Sybil attack
One person pretending to be many people.
Smart contract
Blockchain code that moves assets according to rules.
Threshold encryption
A lock that needs several key holders to cooperate before it opens.

Final assessment

Bottom line

The market-to-court bootstrap is a powerful idea: PredictionMoMo creates open demand, fees, and reality-anchored answer keys, while DemoThemis turns that flow into a wider, better measured, and unbuyable court. Verified humans, sealed draws, receipt-free ballots, private consequences, costed appeals, and constitutional governance reinforce one another.

In one line: an exceptional prediction market feeding bullet proof arbitration that capital cannot buy.
