DemoThemis & PredictionMoMo

The big idea, how it could work, what is already built, and what still needs proof.

Plain-language review Product + security + code + UX Repository and deployed site checked
!
Project status: interactive concept, not a working money app.

The repository contains a polished website, calculators, and simulated journeys. It does not yet contain the real court, prediction market, smart contracts, wallets, World ID connection, private voting system, or live funds.

Thirty-second answer

What is this project?

People lock money around a question. If they disagree about the answer, randomly selected, verified people study the evidence, vote, and decide who receives the money.

Product one

PredictionMoMo

A proposed prediction market where people put money on answers such as YES or NO. Think of it as a shared betting jar whose payout depends on what really happens.

Product two

DemoThemis

A proposed online court. When people disagree, it would ask a randomly chosen group of verified humans to review the evidence and decide the result.

Why together?

Each product helps the other

The market could give the new court cases, fees, and a track record. The court could give the market a neutral way to settle disputed outcomes.

Simple analogy: PredictionMoMo is the game. DemoThemis is the referee. Escrow is the locked prize box that neither side can open until the referee finishes.
Product ideaStrong, unusual, and worth testing.
Interactive explanationClear, polished, and persuasive.
Static websiteHealthy build and small attack surface.
Protocol designInteresting, but several core rules are incomplete.
Real implementationNo contracts, wallet, court, or identity integration yet.
Ready for real money?No. It remains a research prototype.

The problem

Why build a new referee?

Online deals and prediction markets need a trusted answer when people disagree. A company-controlled referee may favor the company. A token vote may give the richest wallets the most power.

DemoThemis tries a different base rule: one verified human can hold one juror seat. World ID is proposed as the proof that a juror is one unique person.

Important limit “One human, one seat” can make it harder to create thousands of fake jurors. It does not prove that a person is honest, skilled, independent, awake, or impossible to pressure.

The proposed journey

How one market could work

  1. Write a clear questionA creator asks something that should later have a YES or NO answer.
  2. Lock money in escrowPeople choose a side. The money goes into a locked software box, not to the winner yet.
  3. Let the shared pool show the oddsThe balance between the two sides becomes the displayed price. Early bets may receive extra weight.
  4. Add fixed-price offersA trader can offer a set price instead of accepting the shared pool’s changing odds.
  5. Graduate a healthy marketIf the market meets proposed liquidity tests, claims become wallet-held YES and NO tokens.
  6. Propose the final answerSomeone posts an answer and risks a bond, which is money they can lose for being wrong.
  7. Watch for a challengeIf nobody objects during the window, the answer pays. A challenged answer goes to court.
  8. Draw verified human jurorsA public random process chooses a panel. A phone-based check is proposed to prove each juror is present.
  9. Review evidence and vote privatelyJurors study the case. The system is meant to reveal only the total, not each person’s vote.
  10. Appeal, finalize, and payA losing side may ask a larger panel to check the result. The final answer unlocks the escrow.

Objective case

Reality can later grade the answer, such as a match score, a closing price, or a delivery date.

Rubric case

Jurors compare evidence with written rules, such as whether work met a contract. There may be no perfect outside answer.

The strongest idea

How the two products could grow together

A new court has a “cold start” problem. It needs work before people trust it, but people may not send work to a court with no history. PredictionMoMo is meant to create the first useful loop.

Markets create cases
Cases create fees
Fees pay jurors
Finished cases build a record
A stronger court attracts markets

Text equivalent: markets → cases → fees → jurors and learning data → trust → more markets.

Proposed fee: 2% at settlement. Of that fee, 70% would pay jurors, 20% would fund longer-term juror rewards, and 10% would pay operating costs. PredictionMoMo may add up to another 1% business margin. These are design numbers, not live fees.

What works well

The project has real strengths

Strong bootstrap idea

The market could give a new court its first cases, income, and public history.

Excellent teaching prototype

The widgets turn difficult ideas into controls a reviewer can explore.

Attacks are discussed openly

Bribery, fake identities, lazy jurors, collusion, appeals, and capture are not ignored.

Wealth is not meant to equal jury power

The court tries to count verified people instead of simply counting tokens.

Healthy static-site engineering

The public build is allowlisted, dependencies are local, and baseline browser headers are present.

Uncertainty is partly visible

The attack model labels many inputs as low-confidence or future assumptions.

Main critique

The most important unanswered questions

These do not mean the idea cannot work. They mean the project must answer them before it can safely hold real money.

01
Resolved in design · implementation must prove it

Can a secret vote remain secret?

The design now keeps each juror’s exact reputation, reserve, payments, penalties, refunds, and case history private. The public sees one-case pseudonyms, eligibility and reserve proofs, the aggregate tally, and aggregate accounting—never a named consequence that could reveal a vote.

Why it mattersA public payment or punishment could become a voting receipt even when the ballot itself stayed encrypted.
What remainsSpecify, implement, and audit the private commitments, batched accounting, aggregate-only tally, and unlinkable payout flow.
Technical detail and evidence

The corrected rule is “public proofs and aggregate accounting; private votes, histories, balances, reputation, penalties, refunds, and payments.” Each case uses a new pseudonym and receiving key. Zero-knowledge proofs reveal only eligibility, a reputation band, sufficient reserve, and correct private updates. Correlation checks must run over encrypted histories and reveal only a proved rule breach. Ordinary threshold encryption still does not automatically force aggregate-only decryption, so the production protocol and its metadata boundary require independent review.

Repository evidence: demothemis.html:493–542, 702–751, 759.

02
Critical · finality risk

Can every case reach a final answer?

Large cases may use several juries and require every jury to agree. One honest disagreement, missing panel, or malicious holdout could stop the money from paying.

Why it mattersAn attacker may not need to control the verdict. Delaying it forever could be cheaper.
What to doDefine quorum, deadlines, replacement rules, how panels combine, and a final fallback that always ends.
Technical detail and evidence

The simulator raises one-panel capture probability to the number of panels. That models how hard it is to capture every panel, but not how easy it may be to cause one disagreement. It also leaves out shared evidence errors, juror outages, abstentions, and correlated mistakes.

Repository evidence: demothemis.html:574–575; game-theory.html:1020–1021.

03
Critical · money and token accounting

Does all the token math balance?

The early market is a shared pot with changing odds and an early-bettor bonus. The later market uses fungible YES and NO tokens. Parlays add another token model. The exact conversion equations are missing.

Why it mattersWithout conservation rules, claims may not remain fully backed or may pay differently from what users expect.
What to doPublish equations and tests for token supply, early weights, redemptions, fees, parlays, rounding, and collateral conservation.
Technical detail and evidence

An older design mints one YES and one NO for one collateral unit, while the current design starts with a weighted parimutuel pool and tokenizes later. A conversion may be possible, but it must prove that no value is created, lost, or promised twice. Composability and lending claims should wait for that proof.

Repository evidence: prediction-market.html:234–355, 386–411, 493–549; prediction-market.md:11–15.

04
Critical · evidence quality

Does the attack model test the idea, or repeat its assumptions?

The simulator is very useful for learning. It is not proof. Some protections succeed because the code assumes they succeed. The named presets are also required to pass the checks.

Why it mattersA score such as 93 out of 100 can look measured even when it is built from uncertain inputs.
What to doAdd unsafe examples, independent assumptions, real measurements, correlated failures, wider attacker ranges, and an outside red team.
Technical detail and evidence

With private ballots enabled, ordinary profitable bribery is prevented by model logic and a residual risk is assigned. The validator rejects any named preset that clears an attack or even reaches a watch state. The model caps attackers and stake sizes, and often treats juror mistakes as independent. Sixteen of 35 assumptions are labeled low-confidence or future rails; seven more are index weights or guardrails.

Repository evidence: game-theory.html:974–1062; assumptions.js; tools/validate-gamelab.js:335–358.

05
High · learning-data risk

How does the system learn the truth?

An answer that nobody challenged is not always true. It may only be small, obscure, or badly timed. Turning unnoticed mistakes into juror test questions could teach the court the wrong lesson.

Why it mattersBad training data can make a confident system consistently wrong.
What to doSeparate court verdicts from outside facts, require evidence quality rules, audit watchers, and define who can create synthetic tests.
Technical detail and evidence

Private markets may also be used as hidden tests, but participants could collude to plant a false answer. Synthetic-test governance is explicitly marked “TBA.” The market token can lower the curation bar even though governance copy says it cannot affect court test data, creating an indirect leak through the claimed firewall.

Repository evidence: demothemis.html:635–695; governance.html:111, 147, 234.

06
High · conflicts and insider knowledge

Can juror conflicts really be found?

A juror may hide an interest in another wallet or through a third party. An attacker may also send tiny unwanted tokens to a juror so that the juror looks conflicted and gets replaced.

Why it mattersReplacement rules can be attacked, while hidden interests may still pass the screen.
What to doUse a pre-draw snapshot, ignore unsolicited dust, hide summons, define exposure rules, and limit juror trading during a case.
Technical detail and evidence

Drawn pseudonyms and wallets are meant to be public for audit, while outcome tokens continue trading during court review. That creates targeting, chain-analysis, hedging, and possible insider-trading problems. Positions may also be hidden through custodians, lending pools, derivatives, or parlays.

Repository evidence: demothemis.html:443, 516, 523–525; prediction-market.html:486–488.

07
High · governance and upgrades

Can the rules be repaired safely?

A truly unchangeable constitution cannot fix a serious bug. An upgradeable constitution needs someone or some process with upgrade power. That power can become another capture risk.

Why it mattersFinancial software needs both strong limits and a safe way to respond to failures.
What to doDefine ownership, guardians, pauses, upgrades, delays, migrations, key custody, user exits, and emergency limits.
Technical detail and evidence

The court token uses square-root token weight, service multipliers, a per-person cap, and a 4% active-weight quorum. The denominator, low-turnout behavior, and manipulation of paid service history need specification. Paying fees to manufacture service is not automatically useless if the governance power gained is worth more than the burn.

Repository evidence: governance.html:153–176, 220, 227–243.

08
High · incomplete state machines

What happens when real life is messy?

The happy path is clear. Many failure paths are not. The rules need an ending for cancelled events, missing jurors, conflicting evidence, broken collateral, and more.

Why it mattersMost serious financial bugs live in unusual transitions, not the normal path.
What to doWrite a complete state diagram and invariants before writing smart contracts.
  • No one proposes an answer, or two people propose different answers.
  • An event is cancelled, delayed, partly complete, or impossible to grade.
  • Too few jurors answer, a juror withdraws, or several panels disagree.
  • Collateral loses value, is frozen, or moves across a failed bridge.
  • An upgrade, pause, migration, refund, or final appeal is required.
Technical detail and evidence

Appeal timing also differs across the site: one place says a full week, another says one month, and governance permits two to eight weeks. Bond sizing, fee rounding, dust, replay, double finalization, and emergency recovery are not specified.

Repository evidence: demothemis.html:755, 764; governance.html:241.

09
High · external dependency

Does World ID provide every required feature?

World ID may help prove that one account belongs to one unique person. The project still needs to prove that draw-time presence, privacy between cases, recovery, reputation, and on-chain checks work together as described.

Why it mattersA provider outage, false rejection, stolen phone, replay, or policy change could stop the court.
What to doBuild a small integration test before treating the exact identity flow as available infrastructure.
Technical detail and evidence

Current World documentation supports privacy-preserving proof of human and on-chain verification. It describes high-assurance Orb-backed uniqueness separately from Selfie Check, which is labeled beta. The repository’s exact mix of persistent eligibility, draw-bound face continuity, unlinkable case proofs, private reputation commitments, and contract enforcement is not demonstrated.

Repository evidence: demothemis.html:470–487. External sources: World ID Overview and On-chain Verification, listed below.

10
High · law and operations

Can it legally and safely handle real money?

Prediction markets, gambling, financial products, customer funds, identity checks, sanctions, and consumer protection have different rules in different countries. “Any topic, any country, no account” is not a safe launch promise.

Why it mattersA technically working market can still be illegal, inaccessible, or unsafe for customers.
What to doChoose one launch region, restrict market types, and obtain specialist legal, compliance, and responsible-gambling advice.
Technical detail and evidence

In the United States, the CFTC says public prediction-market contracts may be swaps or futures and a public market may need designated-contract-market registration. Great Britain requires an appropriate licence to provide remote gambling to British consumers, including when the operator is based abroad. A real service also needs market surveillance, customer-fund controls, AML/sanctions processes, age rules, complaints, incident response, and operational monitoring.

External sources: CFTC 2026 Prediction Markets notice and UK Gambling Commission remote-sector guidance, listed below. This review is not legal advice.

Communication and usability

Product and website critique

Make the message more trustworthy

  • Replace “bulletproof,” “unbuyable,” and “no chance of getting scammed.”
  • Say “designed to raise attack costs under stated assumptions.”
  • Show Built, Simulated, Proposed, and Unproven on every key page.
  • Separate a true five-minute tour from the full technical reference.
  • Add the team, roadmap, grant request, source/spec link, contact route, risks, privacy, and terms.

Make the journeys more honest

  • The 13-event “one market” tour is really a service blueprint with many different actors.
  • Label whether the user is a creator, trader, watcher, juror, loser, or winner.
  • Show quiet settlement before the rare full appeal path.
  • Add failed identity checks, bad wording, missing liquidity, juror timeout, panel disagreement, and cancelled markets.
  • Let reviewers share an exact simulator state through the URL.
The reading-time promise should be corrected. The home page calls six chapters “about a 7 minute tour,” while chapter one alone offers a four-minute overview plus a 35-minute reference. The static prose is already roughly 19,000 words before all generated simulator text.

Accessibility priorities

  • Darken faint text and small green labels so they meet contrast rules.
  • Keep a visible keyboard focus ring on every range slider and button.
  • Use native controls or aria-pressed for selected options.
  • Add aria-current to active navigation and a skip link before repeated menus.
  • Announce only the small result that changed, not an entire rebuilt mock screen.
  • Always pair color with words such as Safe, Warning, or Failed.

Practical next steps

A safer development path

Tell readers the exact status

Clearly label what is built, simulated, proposed, measured, audited, and still unknown. Remove absolute safety claims.

Write one official specification

Define every role, state, deadline, failure path, equation, trust assumption, privacy promise, and upgrade rule. Archive or remove conflicting old drafts.

Test the four hardest ideas separately

Prototype private tally plus slashing; World ID presence plus privacy; pool-to-token accounting; and jury replacement plus guaranteed finality.

Make the simulator adversarial

Add unsafe presets, correlated mistakes, poisoned test data, no-watcher periods, juror denial-of-service, low turnout, and independently chosen assumptions.

Build one small testnet path

One objective binary market, one collateral asset, one seven-person jury, one appeal, and one final payout. No real money.

Seek outside review before real funds

Cryptography, smart contracts, economics, identity, privacy, accessibility, operations, and law all need specialist review.

Defer the extra complexity. Parlays, lending collateral, the court token, private markets, rubric courts, permanent collusion audits, and broad marketplace arbitration should wait until the narrow testnet path works.

Plain words

Short glossary

Prediction market
A place where people put money behind different answers.
Protocol
A shared rulebook carried out by software.
Escrow
Money locked until the rules say who receives it.
Collateral
Money or assets backing a promise or claim.
Token
A digital claim ticket. It is not automatically company ownership.
Assertion
Someone’s proposed final answer.
Challenge
A formal objection to the proposed answer.
Bond
Money risked to discourage dishonest or careless claims.
Juror panel
A small group selected to decide a dispute.
Verified human
A person who has proved uniqueness. This is not proof of honesty or skill.
Private ballot
A vote that should not reveal how one person voted.
Appeal
A request for another, usually larger, panel to check a ruling.
Finality
The point when the decision pays and cannot reopen.
Governance
The process used to change system rules.
Aptitude test
A quiz used to estimate whether a juror understands a topic.
Sybil attack
One person pretending to be many different people.
Smart contract
Blockchain code that moves assets according to rules.
Threshold encryption
A lock that needs several key holders to cooperate before it opens.

Final assessment

Bottom line

DemoThemis and PredictionMoMo form a thoughtful and unusual idea. The strongest part is the loop in which a prediction market gives a new human-jury court its first cases, fees, and learning data. The website explains that idea very well.

Today, however, this is an interactive proposal, not a working or proven financial system. Its hardest questions involve ballot privacy, guaranteed final decisions, token accounting, hidden conflicts, upgrades, identity, and law. The right next step is a small, exact specification and testnet experiment, not stronger safety claims.

In one line: promising idea, excellent explanation, incomplete protocol, and not ready for real money.
